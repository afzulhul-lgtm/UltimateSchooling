Gen Z Credit Score Crisis: 5 Mistakes Hurting Your Credit & How to Fix Them

By Marcus J. Holloway Senior Tech Educator
Published: May 23, 2026 Updated: May 23, 2026 9 Min Read

Gen Z credit score drops 2026 are a growing concern, with many young adults seeing significant declines in their credit scores. But why does this matter? The fact is, a good credit score can be the key to unlocking better loan rates, lower deposits, and even apartment rentals.

And it's not just about the numbers - a good credit score can also be a source of pride and financial stability. So, what's behind the Gen Z credit score drops 2026, and how can young adults start building credit the right way?

Why Gen Z Credit Scores Are Dropping — FICO 2026 Report

According to FICO's Spring 2026 Credit Insights report, 14.4% of consumers aged 18-29 experienced credit score drops of 50 points or more, compared to 10.1% of consumers overall. This is a significant concern, as credit scores play a major role in determining loan rates and creditworthiness. From what's been reported so far, it seems that high credit utilization rates, student loan delinquencies, and thin credit histories are all contributing factors.

Worth noting is that Gen Z consumers are opening credit cards faster than any other age group, which can be both a blessing and a curse. On the one hand, credit cards can be a great way to build credit and earn rewards. On the other hand, they can also lead to overspending and debt if not managed properly. To put that in perspective, the average credit utilization rate for Gen Z is around 44%, which is well above the recommended 30% maximum.

Mistake 1: High Credit Utilization — 44% vs Recommended 30%

One of the biggest mistakes Gen Z consumers are making is carrying high credit utilization rates. As mentioned earlier, the average credit utilization rate for Gen Z is around 44%, which is significantly higher than the recommended 30% maximum. But why is this such a big deal? The truth is, high credit utilization rates can negatively impact credit scores, making it harder to get approved for loans and credit cards in the future.

So, what can Gen Z consumers do to avoid this mistake? For starters, they can try to keep their credit utilization rates below 30% by paying off their balances in full each month. This can be achieved by setting up automatic payments, using budgeting apps, or simply being more mindful of their spending habits. Here's the thing: it's not just about paying off the minimum balance - it's about paying off the entire balance to avoid interest charges and build positive payment history.

Mistake 2: Carrying a Balance — The Myth That Hurts Scores

Another common myth that's hurting Gen Z credit scores is the idea that carrying a balance helps build credit. But that's not entirely clear yet, and it's actually the opposite - carrying a balance only means you pay interest, it doesn't help your credit score. In fact, paying your balance in full each month is the best way to build positive payment history without interest charges.

To put that in perspective, think of it like this: if you have a credit card with a $500 limit, and you carry a balance of $200, you're not building credit - you're just paying interest on that $200. On the other hand, if you pay off that $200 balance in full each month, you're building positive payment history and avoiding interest charges. That's where it gets interesting, because it shows that Gen Z consumers have the power to take control of their credit scores and build a stronger financial future.

Mistake 3: Applying for Too Many Cards at Once

Applying for too many credit cards at once is another mistake that can hurt Gen Z credit scores. When you apply for multiple credit cards in a short period, it can raise red flags with lenders and negatively impact your credit score. So, what can Gen Z consumers do to avoid this mistake? For starters, they can try to space out their credit card applications and only apply for cards that they really need.

And it's not just about the number of credit cards - it's also about the type of credit cards. For example, if you're just starting out, it's better to start with a no-annual-fee credit card and work your way up to more premium cards. That way, you can build credit without breaking the bank. What that means, practically speaking, is that Gen Z consumers need to be more mindful of their credit card applications and only apply for cards that align with their financial goals.

Mistake 4: Student Loan Delinquency — 62-Point Average Drop

Student loan delinquency is another major factor contributing to Gen Z credit score drops. According to FICO data, student loan borrowers with a new delinquency saw an average 62-point credit score drop. This is a significant concern, as student loan delinquencies can have long-lasting impacts on credit scores and financial stability.

But why does this matter? The fact is, student loan delinquencies can be a major setback for Gen Z consumers, making it harder to get approved for loans and credit cards in the future. So, what can Gen Z consumers do to avoid this mistake? For starters, they can try to stay on top of their student loan payments and communicate with their lenders if they're having trouble making payments. That's where it gets interesting, because it shows that Gen Z consumers have the power to take control of their student loan debt and build a stronger financial future.

How Gen Z Can Build Credit the Right Way

So, how can Gen Z consumers build credit the right way? For starters, they can try to start with a no-annual-fee credit card and use it only for budgeted purchases like gas or subscriptions. They can also try to keep their credit utilization rates below 30% by paying off their balances in full each month. And it's not just about credit cards - Gen Z consumers can also try to build credit by making on-time payments on their student loans and other debts.

That's where it gets interesting, because it shows that Gen Z consumers have the power to take control of their credit scores and build a stronger financial future. From what's been reported so far, it seems that Gen Z consumers are eager to learn more about credit and how to build it safely. As a result, financial experts are urging Gen Z consumers to be more mindful of their credit habits and to seek out resources and advice from reputable sources.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Why are Gen Z credit scores dropping in 2026?

A: According to FICO's Spring 2026 Credit Insights report, 14.4% of consumers aged 18-29 experienced credit score drops of 50 points or more, compared to 10.1% of consumers overall. Key factors include high credit utilization rates around 44%, student loan delinquencies after pandemic protections ended, and thin credit histories.

Q: What is a good credit utilization rate?

A: Experts recommend keeping credit utilization below 30%, and those with excellent credit typically have utilization rates below 10%. On a $500 limit card, your balance should be under $150. Gen Z currently averages around 44% utilization.

Q: Does carrying a credit card balance help build credit?

A: No. This is a common misconception. Carrying a balance does not help your credit score — it only means you pay interest. Paying your balance in full each month builds positive payment history without interest charges.

Q: How many points does a student loan delinquency drop your credit score?

A: According to FICO data, student loan borrowers with a new delinquency saw an average 62-point credit score drop. For Gen Z with thin credit histories, the impact can be even more severe.

Q: How can Gen Z start building credit safely?

A: Start with a no-annual-fee credit card, use it only for budgeted purchases like gas or subscriptions, keep utilization under 30%, pay the balance in full monthly, avoid applying for multiple cards at once, and regularly check credit reports for errors.

Q: How many Gen Z consumers have credit score anxiety?

A: According to FICO's report, 60% of Gen Z respondents reported credit score anxiety, and 38% said they would likely open new credit cards as a financial cushion.

Trump Warns Iran Will Be Blown Off the Face of the Earth if It Targets US Vessels

By Marcus J. Holloway Senior Tech Educator
Published: May 05, 2026 Updated: May 13, 2026 7 Min Read

On May 4, 2026 — the first day of a bold US operation to force open the Strait of Hormuz — President Donald Trump issued his starkest warning yet to Iran: attack American ships and Iran would be "blown off the face of the Earth." The threat came as US attack helicopters destroyed six Iranian fast-attack boats in the strait, Iran fired cruise missiles, drones, and rockets at US Navy ships, and Iranian ballistic missiles struck the United Arab Emirates for the first time since the ceasefire took hold in April.

Key Context

The US-Israel war with Iran began on February 28, 2026. A fragile ceasefire took effect in early April. On May 3–5, the launch of Project Freedom — a US push to reopen the Strait of Hormuz — brought the ceasefire to the edge of collapse. Trump paused the operation on May 5 citing progress on an Iran nuclear deal.

Trump's Exact Warning — What He Said

Speaking in a Fox News interview on Monday May 4, Trump made two things unambiguous: the United States would not allow Iran to block the Strait of Hormuz, and the consequences for targeting American vessels would be catastrophic for Iran.

Trump also referenced American military readiness, stating the US had "more weapons and ammunition at a much higher grade than we had before" and that US bases worldwide were fully stocked. He added that the US was "better equipped than ever before" — a message directed as much at US allies watching the confrontation unfold as it was at Tehran.

In a separate interview on ABC News, Trump was asked directly whether the ceasefire with Iran was over and whether a US military strike was imminent. His response: "Well, I can't tell you that." He stopped short of declaring the ceasefire broken, calling the situation "not heavy firing" — a notable effort to manage escalation even as US forces were actively engaged with Iranian boats in the strait.

Trump also called out South Korea specifically, urging Seoul to join Project Freedom: "Perhaps it's time for South Korea to come and join the mission!" — referencing an Iranian attack on a South Korean cargo ship during the operation as justification. Iran had struck the vessel as part of its broader campaign against commercial shipping, which Trump referenced directly in his TruthSocial post announcing the initiative.

What Is Project Freedom?

Trump announced Project Freedom in a lengthy TruthSocial post on Sunday May 3, describing it as a US military operation to guide commercial vessels safely out of the Strait of Hormuz, which Iran has effectively blockaded since the war began on February 28. Hundreds of commercial ships had been stranded in or near the strait, unable to transit due to Iranian threats and mine warnings.

According to US Central Command, the operation deployed:

Asset Detail
Personnel 15,000 US service members
Naval vessels Multiple guided-missile destroyers (12 in the region as of April 24)
Air assets 100+ land and sea-based aircraft
Other platforms Multi-domain unmanned platforms
First result Two US-flagged merchant vessels successfully transited the strait on Day 1

The operation did not involve traditional convoy escorts — US destroyers did not steam alongside individual merchant ships. Instead, CENTCOM described a "broader defensive package" creating a one-way path for commercial vessels exiting the Persian Gulf. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent stated the United States now had "absolute control" over the strait as it worked to reopen the waterway.

According to Axios, citing senior US officials, Trump approved Project Freedom after growing frustrated with what one official described as a "no deal, no war" stalemate — and sought to shift the dynamic by reopening the strait and stripping Iran of its primary leverage over global shipping.

US Destroys Six Iranian Boats: What Happened

Hours after Project Freedom launched, Iran escalated sharply. Iranian forces broadcast over maritime radio channels that "no vessel is permitted to transit the Strait of Hormuz" and that any attempt to do so "will be destroyed." After US warships ignored multiple radio warnings, Iran fired cruise missiles, rockets, and combat drones near US Navy ships, according to Iran's army — statements carried by the semi-official Tasnim news agency.

US forces responded decisively. US attack helicopters destroyed six Iranian fast-attack boats — the small, highly maneuverable vessels Iran has used for decades to harass ships in the Gulf. A seventh was reportedly also engaged; Trump later stated the US had shot down "seven small" Iranian boats. CENTCOM confirmed the strikes and categorically denied that any US vessel had been struck by Iranian missiles: "No U.S. Navy ships have been struck."

Iranian state media initially claimed missiles had hit a US naval vessel near the strait — a claim the US military swiftly rejected. Iran later did not explicitly confirm or deny the engagement.

Iran Strikes the UAE — First Gulf State Attack

In a significant and alarming escalation on the same day, Iranian forces launched a major strike against the United Arab Emirates — the first direct Iranian attack on a Gulf state since the ceasefire took hold in early April.

The UAE Defense Ministry confirmed Iran fired:

The UAE Foreign Ministry condemned the strikes as a "dangerous escalation" and an "unacceptable violation," warning it reserves the "full and legitimate right" to respond. In the immediate aftermath, the UAE shifted schools nationwide to remote learning through the week. Iran denied having plans to target the UAE, with Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi warning both the US and UAE against "being dragged back into quagmire."

How Iran and Its Leaders Responded

Iran's response to Project Freedom and Trump's warning was defiant. Parliamentary speaker and lead negotiator Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf posted on X that while the situation in the strait was clearly "unbearable" for America, Iran has "not even begun yet." Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi wrote that "Project Freedom is Project Deadlock" and that events in the strait make it clear there is "no military solution to a political crisis." Araghchi departed for Beijing on Tuesday — with China, Iran's closest major ally, under mounting international pressure to help end the war.

A warning attributed to Iran's army chief, Major General Amir Hatami, initially circulated on X claiming that US aircraft carriers approaching the strait would be met with force. Iran's semi-official Fars News Agency later said the post came from a fake X account, which was subsequently suspended. However, Iran's Tasnim agency also reported similar comments attributed to Hatami, creating ambiguity about the official Iranian position.

Iran's Revolutionary Guard spokesman Hossein Mohebbi said maritime movements inconsistent with Tehran's "declared principles" would face serious risks. Major General Pilot Ali Abdollahi stated in an IRIB broadcast that "any foreign armed force, especially the aggressive military of America, should they intend to approach or enter the Strait of Hormuz, will be subjected to attack."

Why Trump Paused Project Freedom

On the evening of Tuesday May 5 — just 48 hours after launching Project Freedom — Trump announced the operation was being put on hold, citing progress on Iran nuclear deal negotiations. The abrupt pause surprised many analysts who had interpreted the launch of the operation as a decisive shift in US strategy.

Pakistan, which has served as a key mediator in stalled peace talks, urged both sides to "keep their cool" following the Hormuz confrontation. China, under mounting pressure as Iran's most influential diplomatic partner, had Araghchi traveling to Beijing as the pause was announced. Global oil markets responded with prices retreating but remaining well above $100 a barrel.

Analyst Perspective

Defense analyst Mallory Parker told CNN that Trump was effectively "forcing Iran's hand" — because to stop Project Freedom, Iran would have to directly fire on US warships, representing a different and far more serious level of escalation than targeting commercial shipping. The pause may reflect US negotiating leverage gained from demonstrating willingness to use it.

Global Impact: Oil, Shipping, and the Ceasefire

The Strait of Hormuz carries approximately 20% of the world's oil supply. Iran's effective blockade since February 28 has been the primary driver of Brent crude staying above $100 per barrel and US gas prices climbing to a national average of $4.52 per gallon. The brief, partial opening of Project Freedom — just two US-flagged vessels transiting on Day 1 — was far from the scale needed to meaningfully relieve global shipping pressures.

India, which relies heavily on Gulf oil imports, issued a statement calling for "free and unimpeded navigation and commerce through the Strait of Hormuz in keeping with international law" while emphasizing its support for dialogue. Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif urged both sides to uphold the ceasefire and allow "diplomatic space for dialogue."

The attack on the UAE marked the first time Iran had struck a Gulf state since the April ceasefire, significantly broadening the regional risk map and raising the prospect of additional Gulf Cooperation Council states being drawn into the conflict.

Frequently Asked Questions

What exactly did Trump say about Iran?

In a Fox News interview on May 4, 2026, Trump warned Iranian forces would be "blown off the face of the Earth" if they attempted to target US ships in the Strait of Hormuz or the Persian Gulf. He made the remarks on the same day Project Freedom launched and US forces destroyed six Iranian fast-attack boats.

What is Project Freedom?

Project Freedom is a US military operation announced on May 3, 2026 to guide stranded commercial vessels safely out of the Strait of Hormuz. The operation involves 15,000 service members, guided-missile destroyers, and over 100 aircraft. It was launched on May 4 and paused by Trump on May 5 citing progress on an Iran nuclear deal.

Did the US destroy Iranian boats?

Yes. US attack helicopters destroyed six Iranian fast-attack boats on May 4 after Iran launched cruise missiles, drones, and small boats at US Navy ships and commercial vessels under US protection. Trump later said seven boats were shot down. No US vessels were struck.

Did Iran attack the UAE?

Yes. The UAE Defense Ministry confirmed Iran fired 12 ballistic missiles, 3 cruise missiles, and 4 drones at UAE territory on May 4 — the first direct Iranian attack on a Gulf state since the April ceasefire. The strikes caused three moderate injuries and a major fire at the Fujairah Oil Industry Zone.

Is the US-Iran ceasefire still holding?

It is under severe strain. Despite the exchange of fire, Trump stopped short of declaring the ceasefire broken, describing the situation as "not heavy firing." Iran's Foreign Minister called Project Freedom "Project Deadlock" and said there is "no military solution to a political crisis." Diplomatic talks continue.

Why did Trump pause Project Freedom?

Trump announced the pause on May 5, citing progress on Iran nuclear deal negotiations. According to Axios, he had approved the operation after growing frustrated with a "no deal, no war" stalemate, and the pause may reflect diplomatic leverage gained from the operation's launch.

Conclusion

May 4, 2026 was one of the most consequential single days of the US-Iran conflict. In the space of 24 hours, the United States launched its most ambitious attempt to reopen the Strait of Hormuz since the war began, destroyed Iranian naval assets in direct combat, and saw Iran simultaneously strike a Gulf ally with 19 missiles and drones — all while the ceasefire formally remained in place.

Trump's warning that Iran would be "blown off the face of the Earth" was the clearest statement yet of the administration's red lines — and Iran's defiant response, calling the operation a "deadlock" while warning it had "not even begun," set the stage for a confrontation that pausing Project Freedom has delayed but not resolved.

The Strait of Hormuz remains the central battlefield of this conflict — not just militarily, but economically. With Brent crude above $100, gas at $4.52 per gallon in the US, and hundreds of commercial vessels still unable to move freely, the stakes of reopening or permanently closing the strait extend far beyond the Persian Gulf. How the diplomatic track progresses — particularly China's role in pressuring Tehran — will determine whether Project Freedom's pause becomes a pathway to peace or merely a brief intermission.

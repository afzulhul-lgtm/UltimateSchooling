Veteran Blocked from His Own $4,600 Tax Refund — The 'Prepaid Card' Trap Nobody Warned Him About

By Marcus J. Holloway Senior Tech Educator
Published: May 05, 2026 Updated: May 05, 2026 8 Min Read

A startling case has emerged involving retired US military veteran John Munroe, who found himself completely unable to access his $4,600 federal tax refund — money he desperately needed to cover basic living expenses while dealing with a serious disability and chronic back pain. The cause? A prepaid debit card issued by his tax preparation service that simply would not let him touch the funds, even after the IRS had already deposited them.

This situation is not a freak accident. It is a warning. Thousands of Americans — many of them veterans, elderly individuals, and low-income households — choose prepaid cards for tax refunds every year without fully understanding the risks. Munroe's case puts a human face on a systemic problem that financial regulators have long flagged but that tax preparation chains have been slow to address transparently.

What Happened — John Munroe's Story in Full

John Munroe, a retired US Army veteran living with a service-connected disability and debilitating chronic back pain, visited a Jackson Hewitt Tax Services kiosk located inside his local Walmart to file his annual taxes. When the representative asked how he would like to receive his refund, Munroe explained he did not have his bank account details on hand. The representative suggested a Jackson Hewitt-branded American Express prepaid debit card — presenting it as a quick, convenient solution that would get him his money fast.

Munroe agreed and completed the filing. The IRS processed his return and deposited the full $4,600 refund onto the card. He could see the balance clearly on the mobile app linked to the card. On paper, the money was his. In reality, he could not access a single dollar of it.

When he went to an ATM to withdraw cash, the transaction was declined. He attempted a simple $2 purchase — a smoothie at a gas station — also declined. He tried a $100 transaction at Walmart itself, where he had originally gotten the card. Declined again. Despite the app confirming his balance, every real-world attempt to use the money failed completely.

Critical Warning

A refund appearing in a mobile app does NOT mean you have access to it. Prepaid cards can show a balance while simultaneously blocking all withdrawals and purchases due to compliance holds, deposit limit violations, or card activation failures. Always verify your access method before leaving the tax office.

Munroe, who relies on his tax refund as a significant portion of his annual income due to his disability, was left stranded without the funds he was counting on for rent, medical expenses, and daily necessities. He spent weeks attempting to resolve the issue through the card's customer service line with no resolution — a process advocates describe as deliberately exhausting for vulnerable consumers.

How Prepaid Cards Work for Tax Refunds — IRS Rules Explained

The IRS does permit taxpayers to direct their refunds onto prepaid debit cards, but the rules are specific and non-negotiable. A prepaid card must meet all of the following conditions to legally receive a federal tax refund:

IRS RequirementWhat It Means
Unique Routing NumberThe card must have its own bank routing number, just like a checking account
Unique Account NumberEach card must have an individual account number tied to the cardholder
FDIC Pass-Through InsuranceFunds must be insured by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation up to $250,000
Regulation E ComplianceThe card must offer the same consumer protections as a standard bank account — including error resolution rights and liability limits for unauthorized transactions
No Deposit Limit ExceededThe card must be able to accept the full refund amount — many prepaid cards have hard deposit caps that can block large refunds

The problem is that while these requirements exist on paper, the IRS has no mechanism to verify whether a specific prepaid card actually meets all of them before the deposit is made. Tax preparers may suggest cards that technically carry routing and account numbers but fail on Regulation E compliance, have undisclosed deposit limits, or have activation requirements that were never completed by the taxpayer.

In Munroe's case, the card appeared to satisfy the basic technical requirements — it accepted the deposit — but then failed at every point of actual usage. Consumer advocates believe this indicates a compliance or activation issue that Jackson Hewitt's representatives failed to disclose or check before recommending the card.

Where Things Go Wrong — The Hidden Traps

Prepaid cards marketed by tax preparation companies are designed to look like a seamless, no-bank-needed solution. The reality involves several layers of risk that are rarely explained to taxpayers at the point of sale:

Deposit Caps and Hold Periods: Many prepaid cards have maximum balance limits — sometimes as low as $2,500 — that can trigger automatic holds on deposits exceeding that amount. When a $4,600 refund lands on a card with a $2,500 cap, the card issuer may freeze the excess, the whole balance, or both — with no immediate notification to the cardholder. These holds can last days or weeks while the card company "reviews" the deposit.

Incomplete Card Activation: Some tax preparer-issued cards require secondary activation steps beyond simply receiving the card — identity verification, phone verification, or online account setup. If these steps are not completed, or if the taxpayer is not clearly told they are required, the card may show a balance but block all transactions until activation is finalized. Representatives at tax kiosks often skip this explanation.

ATM Withdrawal Limits: Even fully functional prepaid cards typically impose daily ATM withdrawal limits of $300 to $500. For someone expecting to access $4,600 for rent or medical bills, this is a significant and often undisclosed restriction. Accessing the full balance through an ATM would take weeks of daily withdrawals.

Fee Structures That Drain the Balance: Prepaid cards associated with tax prep services frequently carry fees for ATM withdrawals ($2–$3 per transaction), inactivity fees, balance inquiry fees, and monthly maintenance fees. A taxpayer who is locked out for several weeks while fees accumulate may receive significantly less than their original refund once access is finally restored.

Regulation E Gaps in Practice: Regulation E requires that card issuers investigate and resolve errors — including access failures — within 10 business days. In practice, prepaid card customer service lines are often difficult to reach, documentation requirements are burdensome, and resolution timelines are routinely exceeded. For vulnerable populations like veterans or elderly individuals, navigating this process alone is often impossible.

Expert Tip

Before agreeing to any refund method at a tax preparation office, ask the representative specifically: "What is the maximum daily ATM withdrawal limit? Is there a maximum card balance? Are there any additional activation steps required after I leave?" If they cannot answer all three questions clearly, do not choose that card.

What Both Sides Are Saying

When contacted about Munroe's situation, both Walmart and Jackson Hewitt made statements that were notable not just for what they said, but for what they admitted. Both companies acknowledged that Munroe's case was not isolated — that problems with prepaid cards and tax refunds are a recognized, recurring issue. Both companies also stated that they do not recommend putting tax refunds on prepaid cards. This is a remarkable position for organizations that actively offer and market these cards to taxpayers at the point of filing.

Consumer advocates have pointed out the contradiction sharply: if Jackson Hewitt does not recommend its own branded prepaid cards for tax refunds, why do its representatives in Walmart kiosks suggest them as the default option for customers who lack bank details? The answer, critics argue, lies in the revenue structure. Tax prep companies earn fees from prepaid card usage — per-transaction fees, ATM fees, and monthly maintenance charges that are built into agreements with card issuers. The cards are profitable for the companies even when they are problematic for the customers.

The IRS, for its part, has consistently promoted direct deposit as the safest and fastest refund method, and its official guidance warns taxpayers to verify that any prepaid card they select meets all regulatory requirements before filing. However, the IRS has no authority to regulate the sales practices of private tax preparation companies — a gap that advocates say Congress has been slow to close.

Jackson Hewitt has not publicly issued a formal response to Munroe's specific case beyond general statements. The card issuer — American Express, in this instance operating the prepaid product on behalf of the Jackson Hewitt brand — has its own customer service process, but Munroe reported making multiple attempts to resolve the issue through that channel without success.

What To Do If You Are Already Locked Out

If you find yourself in a situation like Munroe's — where your IRS refund is visible on a prepaid card but inaccessible — there are specific steps you should take immediately:

Step 1 — Contact the Card Issuer Directly: Call the customer service number on the back of your card. Request to speak with a supervisor immediately. Ask for a formal case number and document everything in writing. Under Regulation E, the card issuer must begin investigating an access dispute within 10 business days and resolve it within 45 days, or provisionally credit your account during the investigation.

Step 2 — File a Complaint with the CFPB: The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) accepts complaints about prepaid card issues at consumerfinance.gov/complaint. Filing a complaint creates a formal record and typically prompts a faster response from the card issuer, since companies are required to respond to CFPB complaints within 15 days.

Step 3 — Contact the Taxpayer Advocate Service: The IRS operates a free Taxpayer Advocate Service (TAS) for individuals experiencing financial hardship caused by tax-related issues. If your refund is inaccessible and causing significant financial harm, TAS can intervene on your behalf. Call 1-877-777-4778 or visit taxpayeradvocate.irs.gov.

Step 4 — File a Complaint with Your State Attorney General: State AG offices handle consumer protection complaints against financial service providers. Filing with your state AG adds another layer of pressure and creates a paper trail that may be useful if you pursue legal remedies.

Step 5 — Consult a Consumer Rights Attorney: If the amount is significant (as in Munroe's case, $4,600), a consumer rights attorney can advise you on whether the card issuer has violated Regulation E, and whether you are entitled to damages beyond the refund itself. Many consumer attorneys offer free consultations.

For individuals without a bank account, the best option is not a tax preparer's card — it is to open a basic checking or savings account before filing. Most major banks and all credit unions offer free or low-cost accounts with no minimum balance requirements. The FDIC's BankFind Suite can help locate a bank or credit union near you. Alternatively, the IRS Direct Express card, used by Social Security and disability payment recipients, is a far better-regulated option than prepaid cards sold by tax preparation companies.

How This Affects the Broader Financial Landscape

Munroe's case sits at the intersection of several larger, ongoing debates in American financial policy. The first is financial inclusion — the effort to ensure that individuals without traditional bank accounts have access to safe, reliable financial services. Prepaid cards were originally promoted as a solution to this problem, but recurring access failures like Munroe's demonstrate that poorly regulated prepaid products can actually deepen financial exclusion rather than solve it.

The second is consumer protection in the tax preparation industry. The tax prep industry — dominated by a small number of large companies including H&R Block, TurboTax, and Jackson Hewitt — has faced ongoing scrutiny from the CFPB and Congress for steering low-income customers toward high-fee products. Prepaid refund cards are one element of this, alongside refund anticipation loans and paid filing services for taxpayers who qualify for free filing through the IRS Free File program.

The third is the specific vulnerability of veterans. Many veterans rely heavily on their annual tax refunds as a predictable source of income, particularly those receiving disability compensation that reduces their taxable income but does not replace it entirely. An access failure of several weeks can mean missed rent, skipped medications, and cascading financial consequences that a non-disabled taxpayer with other resources might weather more easily.

Advocates have called on Congress to require tax preparation companies to provide clear, written disclosures of all prepaid card limitations at the point of sale — including deposit caps, withdrawal limits, fee structures, and activation requirements — before a taxpayer can agree to use the card for their refund. Similar disclosure requirements already exist for mortgage products and credit cards; extending them to prepaid refund cards would represent a relatively simple regulatory fix to a problem that affects millions of Americans each tax season.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • Retired veteran John Munroe was completely blocked from accessing his $4,600 IRS tax refund loaded onto a Jackson Hewitt-branded American Express prepaid card — despite the balance showing clearly in the mobile app.
  • The IRS requires prepaid cards to have routing numbers, account numbers, FDIC insurance, and Regulation E compliance — but does not verify these conditions before depositing funds.
  • Prepaid refund cards can fail due to undisclosed deposit caps, incomplete activation requirements, ATM withdrawal limits, and fee structures that drain balances during lockout periods.
  • Both Walmart and Jackson Hewitt acknowledged that Munroe's situation is not isolated — and both stated they do not recommend using prepaid cards for tax refunds, despite marketing them.
  • If locked out, taxpayers should contact the CFPB, the Taxpayer Advocate Service (1-877-777-4778), and their state Attorney General.
  • Direct deposit into a bank or credit union account remains the safest, fastest, and most reliable way to receive a federal tax refund.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I put my tax refund on a prepaid card?

Yes, the IRS allows it — but only if the prepaid card has a unique routing number, a unique account number, FDIC pass-through insurance, and full Regulation E consumer protections. Even when a card technically meets these requirements, access failures remain common due to undisclosed deposit caps and incomplete activation processes. Unless you have no other option, direct deposit into a bank account is strongly recommended.

Why are prepaid cards not recommended for tax refunds?

Because the risks are real and common. Prepaid cards sold by tax preparation companies often carry deposit limits that can trigger holds on large refunds, daily ATM withdrawal caps as low as $300, complex activation requirements that kiosk representatives frequently fail to explain, and fee structures that chip away at your balance during any access delay. Munroe's case — seeing $4,600 in an app while being blocked from every transaction — illustrates what these failures look like in practice.

What are the alternatives to prepaid cards for tax refunds?

Direct deposit into a checking or savings account is the gold standard — fast, safe, and fully protected. If you don't have a bank account, opening one before filing takes as little as 15 minutes online with most major banks. If a bank account is truly not possible, the IRS Direct Express card — used for government benefit payments — is far better regulated than tax preparer cards. A paper check is also a reliable, if slower, option.

How can I ensure I receive my tax refund without issues?

First, use direct deposit and double-check your routing and account numbers before submitting your return. If you must use a prepaid card, ask the preparer to confirm in writing: the maximum card balance, the daily ATM withdrawal limit, all fees, and any activation steps required after receiving the card. Never leave the filing office without completing any required activation steps. And always verify access — make a small test purchase immediately after receiving the card — before assuming the funds are available.

What should I do if I am already locked out of a prepaid card refund?

Act immediately. Contact the card issuer and demand a formal case number. File a complaint with the CFPB at consumerfinance.gov/complaint. Contact the IRS Taxpayer Advocate Service at 1-877-777-4778, which can intervene when refund access is causing financial hardship. File a complaint with your state Attorney General. If the amount is significant, consult a consumer rights attorney — Regulation E violations can entitle you to damages beyond the original refund amount.

Conclusion

John Munroe did everything right. He filed his taxes. He cooperated with the process. He waited for his refund. The system failed him — not because of anything he did wrong, but because a product was sold to him without full disclosure of its limitations, by a company that its own representatives say they do not recommend for this purpose.

His story is important not because it is rare, but because it is not. Every tax season, a significant number of Americans — disproportionately veterans, elderly individuals, and those without traditional bank access — find themselves in similar situations. The funds are there, confirmed by an app, confirmed by the IRS. And they cannot be touched.

The fix is not complicated. Clearer disclosures. Better enforcement of Regulation E timelines. A requirement that prepaid cards be tested for functionality before being recommended to taxpayers. And a broader push to connect unbanked Americans with regulated banking products before tax season begins, rather than funneling them toward high-fee, high-risk alternatives at the point of filing.

Until those changes happen, the most powerful thing any taxpayer can do is know their options before they sit down at the tax preparer's desk — and insist on a safer method than the one being offered.

Influencer Surgeon's Patient Dies During Brazilian Butt Lift — The Dark Reality of Cosmetic Surgery Tourism

By Marcus J. Holloway Senior Tech Educator
Published: May 29, 2026 Updated: May 29, 2026 13 Min Read

On Tuesday, May 26, 2026, Barbara Laura Souza Félix walked into a clinic in Belo Horizonte, Brazil for a procedure she had planned and saved for. She was 27 years old, a saleswoman, and she did not walk back out. Her heart stopped on the operating table during a Brazilian butt lift, and despite more than an hour of resuscitation attempts, she was pronounced dead at 12:20pm. Police opened a homicide investigation. The surgeon responsible — an influencer with 70,000 social media followers — was not working in a hospital. He was renting space in a facility that had already seen a similar death five years earlier.

Barbara's case is not a freak accident. It is the most recent in a pattern that medical professionals, regulators, and patient safety advocates have been warning about for years: the collision of social media culture, cosmetic surgery tourism, and the specific dangers of one particular procedure that the American Society of Plastic Surgeons has identified as having the highest mortality rate of any elective cosmetic operation. Understanding why she died — and why thousands of people every year continue to take the same risk — requires looking honestly at the forces that make the Brazilian butt lift one of the most sought-after and most dangerous surgeries in the world.

Barbara Laura Souza Félix — The Full Story of What Happened

Barbara Laura Souza Félix arrived at the Instituto Mineiro de Obesidade in Belo Horizonte on the morning of May 26, 2026, for a Brazilian butt lift procedure. The surgery was being performed by Dr. Pablo Meneghetti, a cosmetic surgeon who rented operating space at the clinic rather than working within a full hospital system. The procedure involves two phases: first, liposuction to harvest fat from the abdomen or flanks, and second, injection of that fat into the buttocks to augment their size and shape.

During the second phase — the fat grafting — Barbara's condition deteriorated rapidly. Medical staff noticed a sudden drop in her vital signs. Her heart stopped. The surgical team began cardiopulmonary resuscitation immediately and continued for more than an hour. At 12:20pm, she was pronounced dead. She was 27.

Police from the Civil Police of Minas Gerais opened an investigation within hours. The preliminary suspicion, according to investigators, is that Barbara died from a pulmonary fat embolism — a complication specific to the BBL procedure where fat injected into the buttocks enters the gluteal veins and travels through the bloodstream to the lungs, blocking blood flow and causing cardiac arrest. It is the most feared and most deadly complication of the procedure, and it can happen with very little warning.

Barbara's family has called for a full investigation. Dr. Meneghetti's legal team issued a statement indicating that all clinical protocols were followed and that the team did everything possible to save her. Neither the surgeon nor the clinic has provided a statement directly addressing the specifics of what happened during the procedure.

Who Is Dr. Pablo Meneghetti — The Influencer Surgeon

Dr. Pablo Meneghetti built his patient base primarily through social media. With over 70,000 followers on Instagram, he posted before-and-after photographs, procedure clips, and content promoting his cosmetic surgery services to a predominantly young female audience. This model — surgeon as content creator — has become increasingly common in Brazil and in cosmetic surgery markets globally, and it creates a specific dynamic that patient safety experts have flagged repeatedly.

Social media following and surgical competence are entirely unrelated metrics. A surgeon can have 500,000 followers and a history of complications that never appears on their social media feed. Before-and-after photographs are curated by the surgeon. They show selected results. They do not show infection rates, revision rates, or adverse events. The patient scrolling through an Instagram grid of flawless results has no way of knowing what the surgeon's complication rate is from that information alone.

Dr. Meneghetti was not operating within a full hospital — he rented operating space at the Instituto Mineiro de Obesidade, which is classified as a clinic rather than a hospital. This distinction matters significantly in emergencies. A hospital has an immediately accessible ICU, a full anaesthesiology team on staff, and rapid-response cardiac emergency infrastructure. A clinic renting space to independent surgeons may not have equivalent emergency resources available in the minutes that matter when cardiac arrest occurs during surgery.

At the time of publication, the Brazilian Federal Council of Medicine's status on Dr. Meneghetti's registration and any sanctions following Barbara's death has not been publicly reported. The criminal investigation is ongoing.

The Clinic With a History — Instituto Mineiro de Obesidade

The Instituto Mineiro de Obesidade is not encountering serious surgical complications for the first time. In 2021, a 39-year-old mother died of a pulmonary embolism while undergoing surgery at the same facility. That death also involved a cosmetic procedure and the same suspected mechanism: fat or clot blocking blood flow to the lungs. Barbara's death in May 2026 is the second publicly documented fatality at this clinic in five years.

Two deaths from similar causes at the same facility over a five-year period raise questions that Brazilian health authorities will need to answer: what infection control and emergency response protocols does the clinic maintain; what oversight was applied after the 2021 death; what accreditation standards govern the renting of surgical space to independent cosmetic surgeons; and whether the 2021 death was fully investigated and what, if any, recommendations were implemented as a result.

In March 2026, separate from this case, a 31-year-old beautician died in Brazil after undergoing three cosmetic surgeries simultaneously. Performing multiple major procedures in a single operating session significantly extends anaesthesia time and increases the cumulative stress on the cardiovascular system — a practice that mainstream plastic surgery bodies in the UK and US advise against for patients without a specific clinical justification.

What Is a Brazilian Butt Lift — The Procedure Explained

The Brazilian butt lift is a two-phase surgical procedure. Phase one is liposuction: fat is removed from donor areas — typically the abdomen, flanks, lower back, or inner thighs — using a cannula connected to a suction device. The harvested fat is then purified through a centrifuge or filtration process to separate viable fat cells from blood and fluid. Phase two is fat grafting: the purified fat is injected into the buttocks using cannulas to build volume and reshape the contour.

The results, when the procedure is performed correctly by an experienced surgeon in the right facility, can be significant — natural-looking augmentation using the patient's own tissue rather than implants, with the added benefit of contouring the donor area through liposuction. These outcomes drive enormous demand for the procedure. The International Society of Aesthetic Plastic Surgery reported that BBL procedures increased by over 90 percent globally between 2015 and 2022, making it one of the fastest-growing cosmetic surgeries in the world.

The danger is anatomical and specific. The gluteal region is highly vascularised — it has a dense network of large veins. The gluteal veins, particularly the superior and inferior gluteal veins, sit close to the areas where fat is injected. If a cannula breaches the gluteal fascia and fat is injected directly into or near a large vein, fat particles enter the venous bloodstream. They travel to the right side of the heart and then to the pulmonary arteries in the lungs. Fat particles cannot pass through the capillary beds of the lungs. They lodge there, blocking blood flow, causing oxygen to drop, and triggering cardiac arrest.

This is fat embolism syndrome, and it is the mechanism that killed Barbara Laura Souza Félix. It is also what killed dozens of other BBL patients documented in medical literature over the past decade.

Why BBL Is the Most Dangerous Cosmetic Surgery — The Science

The American Society of Plastic Surgeons conducted a multi-centre task force study specifically on BBL mortality and published findings that placed the procedure in a category of its own for elective cosmetic risk. The study found that the estimated mortality rate from BBL in high-risk settings — defined as procedures performed outside fully equipped surgical facilities or by surgeons without specific training in gluteal anatomy — ranged from 1 in 3,000 to 1 in 6,000 procedures.

For context, the mortality rate for a standard facelift is approximately 1 in 250,000. For breast augmentation, it is approximately 1 in 500,000. BBL's mortality rate is 40 to 80 times higher than breast augmentation in comparable settings. No other common elective cosmetic procedure comes close to this risk profile.

The task force's core recommendation, published and updated several times since 2018, is that BBL should only be performed with the injection cannula remaining in the subcutaneous fat layer — above the gluteal fascia — and never passing through into the muscle or deeper tissue where the gluteal veins are located. This requires specific training in gluteal anatomy and ultrasound guidance, which is not universally provided or required.

Surgeons who learned the procedure before the updated guidelines, who trained informally, or who perform high volumes of procedures under time pressure are at higher risk of technical errors that breach the fascia. Ultrasound guidance, which would allow real-time visualization of cannula depth, is not yet standard practice in most markets, including Brazil.

Social Media Surgeons — How Instagram Changed Who Operates on You

The rise of the "influencer surgeon" is a genuinely new phenomenon in medicine, and it has no real precedent in any other medical specialty. A cardiologist does not build their patient base by posting dramatic before-and-after echocardiogram clips. An oncologist does not grow to 200,000 followers by sharing chemotherapy results. But cosmetic surgery, because its outcomes are inherently visual and its market is consumer-facing rather than referral-based, exists in an entirely different relationship with social media.

Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube have become the primary discovery channels for cosmetic surgeons in competitive markets like Brazil, Turkey, the UK, and the US. Surgeons who are skilled at content creation — aesthetically pleasing before-and-afters, personality-driven videos, accessible explanations of procedures — can build audiences that translate directly into patient bookings. The scale of that audience is treated, both by patients and by surgeons themselves, as a proxy for quality. It isn't.

Social media following reflects content creation ability, not surgical safety record. The platforms have no mechanism to display a surgeon's complication rate, their infection rate, their revision rate, or any patient outcomes data. They do have powerful visual tools for displaying the best results from the best cases. The algorithm rewards content that generates engagement — dramatic transformations, enthusiastic patient testimonials — and penalises content that is cautious, nuanced, or risk-focused.

The result is an information environment where a patient choosing a surgeon based on Instagram will systematically receive incomplete information weighted toward positive outcomes and away from risk disclosure. This is not deliberate deception by individual surgeons — it is a structural feature of the platform. But the patient making a life-or-death medical decision based on that information is unaware of how distorted it is.

Cosmetic Surgery Tourism — The Real Risks UK & US Patients Face

Thousands of UK and US patients travel to Brazil, Turkey, and other countries annually for cosmetic procedures, primarily motivated by cost. A BBL in the UK at an accredited surgeon costs between £8,000 and £15,000 depending on the provider and complexity. The same procedure in Turkey can cost £2,000 to £3,500. In Brazil, prices vary enormously — from equivalent to UK rates at private hospitals to a fraction of that at independent clinics or with less established surgeons.

The cost differential is real and the reasons for it are also real: lower labour costs, lower facility overhead, lower insurance costs, and — crucially — lower regulatory oversight in some markets. Not all cheaper procedures involve compromises. Many Brazilian and Turkish plastic surgeons are exceptionally skilled and operate in fully accredited facilities. The cost differential between markets does not automatically indicate lower quality.

But the risk factors that UK and US patients specifically face when seeking surgery abroad include several that are not present when receiving treatment at home:

Credential verification is difficult. In the UK, patients can verify any surgeon's registration with the General Medical Council (GMC) and check whether they are on the Specialist Register for plastic surgery — a specific qualification separate from general surgery. In Brazil, the equivalent body is the Conselho Federal de Medicina (CFM) and the Sociedade Brasileira de Cirurgia Plástica (SBCP). These checks are possible but require knowing how to perform them and the language to navigate the databases.

Post-operative complications emerge after you return home. Many complications from cosmetic surgery — infection, seroma, deep vein thrombosis, wound breakdown — manifest in the days and weeks after surgery, after a patient has returned to their home country. The NHS treats these complications routinely. However, patients may face difficulty receiving treatment for complications arising from overseas procedures, and the UK surgeon managing the complication will be working without the operative notes, implant records, or clinical history from the original procedure.

Flying after surgery increases clot risk. Long-haul flights following cosmetic surgery are associated with significantly increased risk of deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism — the same mechanism that kills BBL patients. Most UK-Brazil flights are approximately 11 hours. Flying within days of a procedure that already carries inherent embolism risk compounds that risk meaningfully.

Important Safety Note

The NHS strongly advises against flying within two weeks of any major cosmetic procedure. For procedures involving fat grafting (BBL), several plastic surgery bodies recommend waiting a minimum of four to six weeks before flying, and obtaining written medical clearance from your surgeon before travel. This advice is frequently not followed by cosmetic surgery tourists operating on tight travel windows.

10 Questions You Must Ask Before Any Cosmetic Surgery

Patient safety advocates and plastic surgery professional bodies have repeatedly published guidance on what questions patients must ask before agreeing to any cosmetic procedure. These are not optional questions for cautious patients — they are the minimum standard of due diligence that any patient should apply before agreeing to elective surgery.

1. Are you board-certified in plastic surgery specifically? General surgery certification is not equivalent to plastic surgery certification. In the UK, look for Fellowship of the Royal College of Surgeons (FRCS) with a plastic surgery subspecialty and entry on the GMC Specialist Register. In Brazil, look for the SBCP credential.

2. How many of this specific procedure have you performed? Volume matters for technical skill. For BBL specifically, high-volume surgeons with dedicated gluteal anatomy training have significantly lower complication rates than lower-volume practitioners.

3. What is your complication rate for this procedure? Any surgeon unwilling or unable to answer this question is a red flag. No surgeon has a zero complication rate. A surgeon who cannot or will not discuss their outcomes data is not giving you the information you need.

4. Where exactly will the surgery take place? The answer should be a fully accredited surgical facility with ICU access, a licensed anaesthetist, and hospital-grade emergency equipment. It should not be a rented clinic room or a converted private space.

5. Who will administer the anaesthesia? The anaesthetist should be a specialist, not a nurse anaesthetist or the surgeon themselves. For a procedure with the cardiac risk profile of BBL, anaesthetic management is critical.

6. What happens if something goes wrong during surgery? Ask specifically what emergency escalation pathway exists. Is there an on-site ICU? Is there a hospital arrangement for immediate transfer? What is the surgeon's protocol if you go into cardiac arrest?

7. What is the full cost, including complications? Some overseas providers quote base prices that do not include anaesthetist fees, hospital facility fees, post-operative garments, or follow-up appointments. Get a complete itemised cost in writing.

8. What aftercare is provided and for how long? A single follow-up appointment the day after surgery is not adequate aftercare for a major procedure. Ask about the full post-operative care protocol.

9. What happens if I have complications after I return home? For overseas surgery, this is the most practically important question. Who will manage your care? How will the UK/US treating doctor access your surgical records?

10. Can I speak to previous patients? Legitimate surgeons with good outcomes can generally provide patient references or connect you with patient communities. Surgeons who are reluctant to allow any patient-to-patient contact should be treated with caution.

Red Flags — Warning Signs of a Dangerous Provider

Beyond the questions above, specific patterns consistently appear in cases where patients have experienced serious complications or death from cosmetic surgery abroad. Recognising these patterns before booking is the most effective form of patient protection available.

Online booking without in-person consultation. Any surgeon who agrees to operate on you without a face-to-face consultation — including assessment of your general health, review of your medications, discussion of your anatomy, and examination of the surgical site — is not following safe practice. Video consultations are better than nothing but are not a substitute for physical examination.

Prices dramatically below the market rate. A significant price reduction compared to the standard market rate in that country almost always reflects a compromise somewhere: less experienced surgeon, less equipped facility, faster surgical time, reduced post-operative care, or some combination. Quality anaesthesia, quality facilities, and experienced surgeons cost money. Prices that seem too good to be true are worth scrutinising carefully.

Pressure to book quickly. Legitimate elective surgery never has a deadline. Time-limited discounts, "last few spots" messaging, and pressure to commit quickly are marketing tactics that work against the patient's interest in making a carefully considered decision.

Combining multiple major procedures in one session. Offering to perform a tummy tuck, BBL, and breast augmentation in a single operating session may seem efficient, but it dramatically extends anaesthesia time and cumulative surgical stress. The risk of complications increases non-linearly with operating time. Each additional major procedure is not simply additive — the risk compounds.

No hospital admitting privileges. Ask directly whether the surgeon has admitting privileges at a local hospital. A surgeon who cannot admit patients to a hospital in an emergency is a surgeon whose patients, if they deteriorate, must be managed through emergency room systems without the surgical team's involvement.

Social media presence used as primary credentialing. Having followers is not a surgical qualification. Curated Instagram results are not outcome data. If the primary evidence of a surgeon's quality is their social media presence rather than their credentials, peer recognition, facility accreditation, and transparent outcome data, treat that as a warning sign.

Conclusion

Barbara Laura Souza Félix was 27 years old. She had a life, a job, a family, and plans. She died in an operating room during an elective procedure while the surgeon who was supposed to protect her rented space in a building that had already seen the same kind of death five years before. The investigation into exactly what went wrong will take time. The broader pattern that her death represents has been documented, warned about, and studied for years — and it continues because the forces that drive patients toward high-risk situations are not primarily medical. They are social, financial, and algorithmic.

Social media creates surgeons who look credible because they have audiences. Cost differentials create situations where patients choose providers partly on price. The intersection of these two dynamics produces a market where patients routinely lack the information they need to make genuinely informed decisions. The questions and red flags outlined in this article are not sophisticated knowledge — they are basic due diligence that, if applied systematically, would protect many of the patients who are harmed every year by providers who should never have been trusted with their care.

If you are considering a BBL or any other cosmetic procedure, please read those questions again. Ask every one of them. If you do not receive clear, confident, documented answers to all of them, do not proceed with that provider. The procedure, the recovery, the result — none of it is worth what Barbara Laura Souza Félix's family is now living with.

US Sinks Iranian Boats and Shoots Down Missiles as Navy Forces Open Strait of Hormuz

On the first day of Project Freedom — the Trump administration's push to reopen the Strait of Hormuz — US Navy attack helicopters destroyed six Iranian fast-attack boats, intercepted multiple Iranian cruise missiles, and shot down drones launched against US ships and commercial vessels under American protection. It was the most direct US-Iran naval combat since the conflict began on February 28, 2026.

US Central Command (CENTCOM) confirmed the engagements in detail. No US Navy ships were struck. Two US-flagged merchant vessels became the first commercial ships to transit the strait since Iran effectively closed it at the start of the conflict. The operation marked a decisive military shift — but was paused just 24 hours later when Trump cited progress on Iran nuclear deal negotiations.

What Happened on May 4 — Hour by Hour

Project Freedom officially began on the morning of Monday May 4, with US CENTCOM announcing it would use guided-missile destroyers, over 100 land and sea-based aircraft, and multi-domain unmanned platforms to create a protected corridor for commercial shipping exiting the Persian Gulf through the Strait of Hormuz.

Iran's response was immediate. The Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps Navy (IRGCN) broadcast warnings over maritime radio channels stating that "no vessel is permitted to transit the Strait of Hormuz" and that any attempt to do so "will be destroyed." After US warships ignored the warnings and continued the operation, Iran escalated to direct fire.

According to CENTCOM commander Admiral Brad Cooper, Iranian forces launched a coordinated attack involving cruise missiles, rockets, combat drones, and fast-attack boats against US Navy ships and commercial vessels being protected under the operation. US forces engaged all incoming threats. Attack helicopters destroyed the Iranian boats. Electronic warfare and air defense systems intercepted the missiles and drones. The engagements were described by Cooper as conducted through the "clinical application of defensive munitions."

CENTCOM Commander Brad Cooper's Full Account

Admiral Brad Cooper, head of US Central Command, gave the most detailed public accounting of the Day 1 engagements. His statements — made to media on the evening of May 4 — are the primary authoritative source for what happened:

On the Iranian attack: "The IRGC has launched multiple cruise missiles, drones and small boats at ships we are protecting. We have defeated each and every one of those threats through the clinical application of defensive munitions."

On the nature of the defensive operation — and why it differs from traditional convoy escort: "If you're escorting a ship, you're playing kind of one on one. I think we have a much better defensive arrangement in this process." Cooper explained that instead of US destroyers flanking individual commercial vessels, the operation created a broader defensive corridor — a one-way path allowing ships to exit the Persian Gulf while US forces maintained area defense.

On outreach to the shipping industry: "Over the last 12 hours, we've reached out to dozens of ships and shipping companies to encourage traffic flow through the strait. This news has been quite enthusiastically received, and we're already beginning to see movement."

On the US naval blockade of Iranian ports: Cooper confirmed it was "exceeding expectations" — a separate and simultaneous pressure campaign that has been in effect since April 13.

How the Iranian Boats Were Destroyed

The six Iranian fast-attack boats were destroyed by US Apache and Seahawk attack helicopters operating from US Navy destroyers in the strait. Both aircraft types are well-suited for this mission: the Apache carries Hellfire missiles and a 30mm cannon, while the MH-60 Seahawk is the Navy's primary anti-surface warfare helicopter, capable of firing Penguin or Hellfire missiles at fast-moving surface targets.

Fast-attack boats — known in military terminology as Fast Attack Craft (FAC) — have been a cornerstone of Iranian naval strategy since the Tanker War of the late 1980s. Iran operates hundreds of these vessels through the IRGCN, designed to swarm larger targets in the shallow, confined waters of the Persian Gulf where the maneuverability advantage of large warships is reduced. They have been used to harass, threaten, and mine shipping for decades.

In open water encounters, attack helicopters represent an effective counter to fast-attack boat swarms: they are fast enough to intercept, precise enough to target individual boats, and can operate ahead of the convoy being protected. The Day 1 engagements demonstrated this counter effectively — six boats destroyed with no damage to US vessels.

The Mine Threat: Clearing a Path Through the Strait

One of the less-reported but strategically significant elements of Day 1 was the US effort to clear Iranian sea mines from the Strait of Hormuz. CENTCOM advisory notices to mariners had already warned vessels that usual routes should be considered "extremely hazardous due to the presence of mines that have not been fully surveyed and mitigated," recommending ships consider routing through Omani territorial waters instead.

Cooper confirmed US forces were actively working to clear a pathway through the strait. Iran has historically used sea mines as an asymmetric weapon against larger naval forces — it mined the Persian Gulf during the Tanker War, resulting in damage to multiple vessels including the USS Samuel B. Roberts in 1988. The presence of unsurveyed mines added a significant layer of risk to any commercial vessel attempting the crossing, even beyond the direct threat of Iranian attack.

Iran's navy also published a map claiming to show the area of the strait under its control — though it was not publicly confirmed whether this represented any formal change to their declared positions or was primarily a signaling measure.

The Two Ships That Made It Through

Despite the Iranian attacks, US forces achieved the primary Day 1 objective of Project Freedom: two US-flagged merchant vessels successfully transited the Strait of Hormuz under US military protection — the first commercial ships to do so since Iran effectively sealed the waterway at the start of the conflict on February 28.

Iran denied the crossings had occurred. The IRGCN claimed no commercial vessels had transited the strait and called US claims false. CENTCOM's statement confirmed the transits happened. Independent maritime tracking agencies subsequently confirmed movement consistent with the US account.

The symbolic and practical significance of even two vessels making it through was substantial. With hundreds of commercial ships stranded in or near the Gulf — unable to load or offload cargo, burning through operating costs with no revenue — any proof-of-concept transit was intended to signal to the global shipping industry that movement was possible under US protection.

Shipping Industry Context

Cooper reported that US forces had contacted dozens of ships and shipping companies in the 12 hours before Day 1 to encourage traffic through the strait. He described the response as "quite enthusiastically received" — reflecting the enormous pent-up demand from vessels that had been trapped in the Gulf for weeks with cargo they could not move and costs mounting daily.

Iran's Denial and Counter-Claims

Iran's official response to Day 1 of Project Freedom involved a combination of outright denial and continued defiance. The IRGCN categorically denied that any commercial vessels had transited the strait. Iranian state media initially claimed that Iranian missiles had struck a US naval vessel near the strait — a claim CENTCOM rejected immediately and categorically: "No U.S. Navy ships have been struck."

Major General Pilot Ali Abdollahi issued a statement carried by Iran's state TV channel IRIB warning that "any foreign armed force, especially the aggressive military of America, should they intend to approach or enter the Strait of Hormuz, will be subjected to attack." IRGCN spokesman Hossein Mohebbi added that maritime movements inconsistent with Tehran's "declared principles" would face serious risks.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi took a more measured but equally firm line on X, writing: "Project Freedom is Project Deadlock" and stating that events in the strait make it clear there is "no military solution to a political crisis."

Why the Strait of Hormuz Matters So Much

The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most important oil chokepoint. At its narrowest, it is only 33 kilometers wide, with two-mile-wide shipping lanes in either direction. Through this narrow channel passes approximately:

CommodityShare of Global SupplyDaily Volume
Crude oil and petroleum products ~20% of global supply ~17–20 million barrels/day
Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) ~25% of global LNG trade Significant Qatar/UAE exports
Total commercial vessel transits — ~20–30 vessels/day (pre-blockade)

Iran's effective blockade since February 28 had already pushed Brent crude above $100 per barrel and driven US gas prices to a national average of $4.52 per gallon. The longer the strait remained closed, the more severe the economic damage to importing nations — including major Asian economies like India, China, Japan, and South Korea, all of which rely heavily on Gulf oil. This is why Project Freedom had support across the political spectrum internationally, even from nations that otherwise prefer diplomatic resolution.

The Separate US Naval Blockade of Iran

Running in parallel to Project Freedom is a separate and distinct US military operation: the naval blockade of Iranian ports, in effect since April 13, 2026. This operation prevents ships from entering or departing Iranian territory — applying direct economic pressure on Tehran by cutting off its own imports and exports.

Admiral Cooper stated the blockade was "exceeding expectations." While the blockade and Project Freedom are operationally separate — the blockade targets Iranian ports, while Project Freedom targeted the transit corridor for non-Iranian commercial shipping — together they form a two-pronged maritime strategy: open the strait for the world while closing it economically for Iran.

Conclusion

Day 1 of Project Freedom was a military success on its own terms: every Iranian threat was defeated, no US ships were hit, and two commercial vessels made it through a waterway that had been closed for over two months. The operation demonstrated that the US Navy, with its attack helicopter capability and layered air defense, could protect commercial shipping against Iran's asymmetric naval tactics — including fast-attack boat swarms, cruise missiles, and drones simultaneously.

But the pause on Day 2 highlighted the fundamental tension at the heart of US strategy. Project Freedom was designed to strip Iran of its economic leverage over global shipping. The moment it succeeded — even partially — it gave both sides an incentive to negotiate. Whether the pause translates into a durable agreement on the strait or simply allows Iran to regroup and reinforce its blockade capabilities will determine whether the ships that did not get through on May 4 will ever have the chance to try again.

The Strait of Hormuz remains the single most consequential geographic chokepoint of this conflict. What happens there in the coming weeks will shape oil prices, global supply chains, and the trajectory of the US-Iran confrontation more than any other single factor.

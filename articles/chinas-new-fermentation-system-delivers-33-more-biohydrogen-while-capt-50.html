China's new fermentation system delivers 33% more biohydrogen while capturing carbon dioxide

Researchers from the Institute of Applied Ecology at the Chinese Academy of Sciences have developed a fermentation system that increases biohydrogen production by 33% while simultaneously capturing carbon dioxide — without any separate carbon capture equipment. The key is a single mineral additive called wollastonite, which acts as both a pH buffer and a CO2 mineralizer inside the fermentation reactor itself.

The study demonstrates that it is possible to produce green hydrogen with a negative carbon footprint using widely available organic waste as feedstock. The findings were published in May 2026 and have quickly drawn attention from clean energy researchers worldwide.

How Dark Fermentation Works

Dark fermentation is a biological process that breaks down organic materials — such as food waste, agricultural residue, or glucose — to produce hydrogen gas under oxygen-free (anaerobic) conditions. It does not require sunlight, making it more practical and scalable than photo-biological hydrogen production methods.

The process relies on anaerobic bacteria, particularly strains of Clostridium, which metabolize organic substrates and release hydrogen as a byproduct. However, dark fermentation has a built-in self-limiting problem: as bacteria work, they produce volatile fatty acids (VFAs) as metabolic byproducts. These acids accumulate and drop the system pH — sometimes as low as 4.5 — which suppresses microbial activity and slows or halts hydrogen production. Maintaining a stable pH between 6.5 and 7.0 is critical for sustained, high-yield fermentation.

Traditional solutions involve adding chemical pH buffers like sodium bicarbonate, but these can introduce sodium toxicity and add cost. The Chinese research team took a different approach: a naturally occurring mineral that buffers pH automatically in response to acid accumulation.

Wollastonite as a Dual-Function Agent

Wollastonite (CaSiO₃) is a calcium silicate mineral found naturally in metamorphic rocks. It is already used industrially in ceramics and construction materials, making it affordable and widely available. The researchers' key insight was that wollastonite reacts specifically with acidic environments — exactly the conditions that form inside a fermentation reactor.

As volatile fatty acids accumulate during fermentation, they dissolve the wollastonite mineral. This reaction consumes protons (the source of acidity) and releases calcium ions (Ca²⁺) into the liquid medium. The result is continuous, self-regulating pH buffering — the more acid is produced, the more wollastonite dissolves, and the more the pH is corrected. This stabilizes fermentation conditions at the optimal range of pH 6.5–7.0 without any external intervention.

The released calcium ions then serve a second function: they react with dissolved CO₂ in the fermentation liquid to precipitate it as calcite (CaCO₃) — a stable, solid mineral. This is in-situ carbon capture, happening inside the reactor at the same time as hydrogen is being produced.

The Numbers: Yield and Lag Phase Improvement

The performance gains from adding wollastonite at the optimal dosage of 10 g/L were significant across two key metrics:

Metric Without Wollastonite With Wollastonite (10 g/L) Improvement
Hydrogen Yield (mL/g glucose) 158.11 ± 3.44 210.75 ± 15.87 +33%
Lag Phase Duration (hours) 23.13 hours 12.38 hours ~50% shorter
System pH Range Drops to ~4.5 Stabilized at 6.5–7.0 Fully controlled

The lag phase — the initial period before active hydrogen production begins — was cut nearly in half. A shorter lag phase means bacteria reach peak productivity faster, which is directly relevant to the economics of any industrial biohydrogen facility.

The improved hydrogen yield is driven by a shift in the microbial community. Stabilized pH conditions favor the growth of hydrogen-producing Clostridium bacteria while suppressing Lactobacillus, which competes for the same substrate but produces lactic acid instead of hydrogen. The result is a cleaner, more hydrogen-focused fermentation process.

Key Takeaways

  • Wollastonite at 10 g/L increased biohydrogen yield by 33% — from 158.11 to 210.75 mL/g glucose.
  • The lag phase was cut from 23.13 to 12.38 hours, a roughly 50% reduction that speeds up production start.
  • The same mineral simultaneously captures CO₂ by precipitating it as solid calcite (CaCO₃) inside the reactor.
  • A two-stage approach resolves the conflict between maximum hydrogen yield and maximum CO₂ capture.
  • The two-stage system captures up to 0.49 L of CO₂ per liter of medium and enriches hydrogen purity to 58.2%.

The CO2 Capture vs. Hydrogen Yield Conflict

The researchers quickly identified a tradeoff: the optimal dosage for hydrogen production (10 g/L) and the optimal dosage for maximum CO₂ capture are not the same. At higher dosages of wollastonite — ≥15 g/L — the system captures more CO₂, but the excess calcium and the passively elevated pH begin to interfere with microbial activity, reducing hydrogen yield.

This created a design challenge: simply adding more wollastonite was not the answer. Running the system at 10 g/L maximized hydrogen but left CO₂ capture below its potential. The team needed a way to get the best of both — peak hydrogen production and thorough carbon capture.

Key Insight

More wollastonite is not always better. At doses above 10 g/L, CO₂ capture increases but hydrogen yield drops. The solution is a two-stage process that separates and optimizes both goals sequentially.

The Two-Stage Solution

To resolve this conflict, the researchers developed a two-stage strategy that sequences hydrogen production and carbon capture rather than trying to maximize both at once:

Stage 1 — Maximize Hydrogen: The fermentation runs with 10 g/L wollastonite under standard conditions. This produces the highest possible hydrogen yield while still providing significant passive CO₂ capture as a byproduct of the mineral's buffering action.

Stage 2 — Maximize CO₂ Capture: After fermentation is complete, the system pH is adjusted to 7.0. At neutral pH, the dissolved calcium ions remaining in the liquid react more aggressively with any remaining dissolved CO₂, precipitating it as additional calcite. This post-fermentation step captures up to 0.49 liters of CO₂ per liter of medium without affecting the hydrogen that has already been produced.

An important secondary benefit of Stage 2 is that removing dissolved CO₂ from the gas mixture enriches the hydrogen gas purity to 58.2%. Purer hydrogen is easier and cheaper to process for use in fuel cells or industrial applications, improving the overall economics of the system.

Why This Research Matters

Most current methods of producing hydrogen — including steam methane reforming, the dominant industrial process — are carbon-intensive. Green hydrogen produced via electrolysis from renewable electricity is clean but expensive. Biohydrogen from dark fermentation occupies a promising middle ground: it uses organic waste as a feedstock, it is biologically driven rather than electricity-intensive, and it can run continuously.

The addition of wollastonite transforms dark fermentation from a carbon-neutral process into a potentially carbon-negative one — meaning it removes more CO₂ from circulation than it emits. For countries with large volumes of agricultural or food waste, this could represent a meaningful pathway to both clean energy and emissions reduction simultaneously.

The use of a low-cost, naturally abundant mineral — rather than engineered catalysts or expensive chemicals — also keeps the system practical for real-world scale-up. Wollastonite is already mined commercially and costs a fraction of purpose-built carbon capture materials.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the new biohydrogen fermentation method developed by Chinese scientists?

Researchers from the Institute of Applied Ecology at the Chinese Academy of Sciences introduced wollastonite (CaSiO₃) as a dual-function additive in dark fermentation. It boosts hydrogen production by 33% and simultaneously captures CO₂ by precipitating it as stable calcite, achieving what researchers describe as negative-carbon biohydrogen.

How does wollastonite improve biohydrogen production?

As acids accumulate during fermentation, they dissolve the wollastonite, consuming protons and releasing calcium ions. This provides continuous, self-regulating pH buffering that keeps the system at pH 6.5–7.0 — the optimal range for hydrogen-producing Clostridium bacteria. It also suppresses competing Lactobacillus bacteria that reduce efficiency.

What is the optimal dosage of wollastonite for hydrogen yield?

The optimal dosage is 10 g/L. At this level, the lag phase shortened from 23.13 to 12.38 hours — roughly a 50% reduction — and hydrogen yield increased from 158.11 ± 3.44 mL/g to 210.75 ± 15.87 mL/g of glucose consumed.

How does the system capture CO₂?

What is the two-stage strategy for biohydrogen and carbon capture?

Is wollastonite expensive or difficult to source?

No. Wollastonite is a naturally occurring mineral that is already mined and used commercially in ceramics, plastics, and construction materials. It is widely available and low-cost compared to engineered carbon capture materials, which makes this approach more viable for industrial scale-up.

Conclusion

The Chinese Academy of Sciences research demonstrates that a single, inexpensive mineral additive can simultaneously solve two of dark fermentation's biggest problems — unstable pH and CO₂ emissions — while meaningfully increasing hydrogen output. The 33% yield improvement and the near-50% reduction in lag phase are both practically significant numbers for anyone designing a biohydrogen production facility.

The two-stage strategy is an elegant solution to the tradeoff between hydrogen yield and CO₂ capture, allowing both to be maximized without compromising the other. Combined with the use of low-cost, naturally abundant wollastonite as the key agent, this system has a realistic path toward commercial viability.

As global interest in clean hydrogen accelerates, breakthroughs that lower production costs and add carbon removal benefits will be increasingly valuable. This research represents one of the more practical advances in biohydrogen science published in 2026 — and one worth watching as it moves toward pilot-scale testing.

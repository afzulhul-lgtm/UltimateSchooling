Apple WWDC 2026 Dates Confirmed (June 8–12) — iOS 20, macOS 16 & Everything Expected

By Marcus J. Holloway Senior Tech Educator
Published: May 19, 2026 Updated: May 19, 2026 7 Min Read

Apple has officially confirmed that WWDC 2026 — the Worldwide Developers Conference — will run from June 8 to June 12, 2026, with the main keynote on June 8 at 10 am PT (1 pm ET / 6 pm BST). The announcement dropped on May 18, one day before Google I/O 2026's keynote — a timing that was almost certainly deliberate. Apple wanted developer and media attention on June 8 while the tech press was already in conference mode for Google's event on May 19.

WWDC 2026 is expected to be one of Apple's most significant software years in recent memory. iOS 20 and macOS 16 are both up for reveal, Apple Intelligence is due for a major expansion, and there are credible reports of deeper third-party AI integrations across the entire platform ecosystem. For developers, the five days of labs and sessions following the keynote are where the real work happens — and 2026 looks like a year where the session catalog will be dense with new APIs.

The keynote will stream live on apple.com/apple-events, Apple's YouTube channel, and the Apple TV app. No tickets are required for remote viewing. In-person developer lab access requires registration through the Apple Developer portal — Apple has operated WWDC as a hybrid event since 2021 and that format continues in 2026.

The Platforms State of the Union, which follows the public keynote, is the technical companion session aimed at developers. It typically runs 90 to 120 minutes and dives into the API and framework changes that will dominate the subsequent four days of labs. Developers who only watch one session beyond the keynote should watch the State of the Union — it's where the practical implications for app development become clear.

What Apple Is Expected to Announce at WWDC 2026

WWDC is a software developer conference, so hardware announcements are rare — but not impossible. The 2023 WWDC introduced the Vision Pro, breaking the no-hardware tradition in a significant way. For 2026, no major hardware reveal is expected, but the software lineup is substantial enough to dominate the headlines on its own.

The core expected announcements:

  • iOS 20 — The biggest annual release. AI-native redesigns of core apps, expanded Apple Intelligence features, and rumored changes to the home screen and notification system.
  • macOS 16 — Following the pattern of each macOS being named after a California location, macOS 16 is expected to bring major Apple Silicon optimization improvements and tighter iPhone-to-Mac continuity features.
  • visionOS 3 — Apple Vision Pro's third software generation, with expanded spatial computing APIs and broader third-party app support. Developer engagement with visionOS has been growing steadily since launch.
  • watchOS 13 — Health monitoring improvements, new fitness metrics, and likely deeper Apple Intelligence integration for on-wrist Siri responses.
  • iPadOS 20 — Expected to bring additional pro-level multitasking features and closer feature parity with macOS, addressing long-standing developer and power-user complaints about iPadOS limitations.

iOS 20: What Developers and Users Should Expect

iOS 20 is the version number that actually breaks the symmetry — iOS ended its teens at iOS 19 last year, and iOS 20 carries the same kind of psychological weight that Windows 10 or Android 10 did at their respective launches. Apple is expected to use the round number as an opportunity to introduce a more substantial visual and structural refresh than typical annual updates.

The AI story is central. Apple Intelligence, introduced in iOS 18 and expanded in iOS 19, is expected to receive significantly broader capabilities in iOS 20, including:

  • Context-aware Siri that maintains conversation history across apps and sessions
  • On-device AI writing tools expanded to all text fields system-wide
  • Third-party developer API access to Apple Intelligence models — the feature developers have been waiting for most since Apple Intelligence launched
  • Visual Intelligence improvements that bring real-time object and text recognition to more system contexts

On the UI side, credible reports from leakers including Mark Gurman at Bloomberg have pointed to changes in the home screen layout system, giving users more flexibility in widget and app arrangement than the current fixed-grid model. Whether this is a minor tweak or a more fundamental redesign will become clear on June 8.

Key Takeaways

  • Expected announcements: iOS 20, macOS 16, visionOS 3, watchOS 13, iPadOS 20 — all with expanded Apple Intelligence AI features and a major new third-party developer API for Apple's on-device AI models.
  • Apple announced WWDC 2026 on May 18 — one day before Google I/O 2026 on May 19 — in what appears to be a deliberate move to compete for developer and media attention during Google's biggest event of the year.

macOS 16, visionOS 3 & watchOS 13 Previews

macOS 16's most anticipated feature is improved Apple Silicon performance optimization. The M4 Ultra chip has been widely deployed in Mac Studio and Mac Pro configurations, and macOS 16 is expected to unlock additional core-scheduling efficiency improvements that take advantage of the chip's architecture in ways macOS 15 didn't fully exploit. Developers building compute-intensive apps — video editing, 3D rendering, machine learning workloads — are watching this closely.

The continuity story between iPhone and Mac is also expected to advance. iPhone Mirroring, introduced in macOS 15, could expand to allow two-way interaction — currently, you can control your iPhone from your Mac, but the reverse isn't fully supported. A bidirectional continuity system would make the iPhone-to-Mac workflow meaningfully more fluid for professionals switching between devices.

For visionOS 3, the developer API story is the primary focus. Apple Vision Pro has approximately 2,000 native visionOS apps as of early 2026 — a solid start but well below the app density that makes the platform truly useful as a daily productivity tool. Apple is expected to introduce spatial development tools that lower the barrier for developers who currently only maintain iOS versions of their apps.

The platform is Google's answer to visionOS — an operating system purpose-built for spatial computing devices rather than adapted from a phone OS. Samsung's first Android XR-powered glasses, reportedly developed under the codename "Project Moohan 2," are expected to make their public debut at Google I/O 2026 as a demonstration platform for what Android XR can do.

WWDC 2026 vs Google I/O 2026: The AI Platform Battle

The timing of Apple's WWDC announcement — dropped on May 18, the day before Google I/O — makes explicit what has been building for two years: the Apple-Google rivalry has shifted from hardware to AI platforms. Both companies are fighting for developer attention, and the battleground is whose AI infrastructure developers choose to build on.

Apple's position: on-device AI that runs privately, integrates deeply with the operating system, and doesn't require internet connectivity. The target developer is someone building consumer-facing apps where user trust and privacy are selling points. The limitation has been that Apple's AI APIs have been closed to third-party developers — a gap iOS 20 is expected to close.

Google's position: cloud-scale Gemini models that can do things no on-device model can match, combined with Android's open developer ecosystem. The target developer is someone building AI-native apps that need real-time information, multimodal reasoning, or enterprise integrations. The limitation is that cloud AI raises privacy concerns and requires connectivity.

Neither position is clearly superior — they're genuinely different tradeoffs for different applications. What 2026 represents is the first year where both companies are competing directly for the same developer attention at the same time of year, with Apple specifically choosing to frame its announcement in the context of Google's event. That competitive posture is new and will define how the developer community reads both keynotes in the coming weeks.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is Apple WWDC 2026?

Apple WWDC 2026 runs from June 8 to June 12, 2026. The main keynote is on June 8 at 10 am PT (1 pm ET / 6 pm BST). Developer labs, video sessions, and the Apple Design Awards continue through June 12.

What is Apple announcing at WWDC 2026?

Apple is expected to announce iOS 20, macOS 16, visionOS 3, watchOS 13, and iPadOS 20. The central theme is Apple Intelligence — specifically expanded AI features across all platforms and the first developer API allowing third-party apps to directly access Apple's on-device AI models.

What is the full WWDC 2026 schedule?

June 8: Main keynote at 10am PT followed by the Platforms State of the Union. June 9–12: On-demand video sessions, in-person developer labs (by registration), design reviews, Swift Student Challenge awards, and Apple Design Awards.

When is Google I/O 2026?

Google I/O 2026 keynote is on May 19, 2026 at 1 pm ET. Major expected announcements include Android 17, Gemini Ultra 2.0, Android XR for smart glasses, and Samsung's first XR glasses demonstration.

What is Android XR?

Android XR is Google's extended reality operating system designed specifically for AR glasses and mixed reality headsets. Unlike Android, which was adapted from a phone OS for larger screens, Android XR was built from the ground up for spatial computing. Samsung's first smart glasses are expected to run Android XR and may be revealed at Google I/O 2026.

How can I watch WWDC 2026 and Google I/O 2026?

WWDC 2026 streams live at apple.com/apple-events, on Apple's YouTube channel, and in the Apple TV app on June 8 at 10am PT. Google I/O 2026 streams live today, May 19 at 1pm ET, on the Google I/O website and Google's official YouTube channel.

Conclusion

Apple WWDC 2026 is shaping up to be one of the most developer-relevant conferences in years — not because of hardware drama, but because of what iOS 20 is expected to deliver on the AI front. Third-party developer access to Apple Intelligence models, if it arrives as expected on June 8, will fundamentally change what iOS developers can build. That's the headline that matters most to the people who will spend June 8–12 in Apple's developer labs.

The Google I/O 2026 happening today adds context to everything Apple is about to announce. These two companies are making competing bets on what AI-native software looks like — Apple betting on private, on-device intelligence, Google betting on cloud-scale reasoning and open developer tools. Watching both keynotes back-to-back this week is the clearest possible picture of where the platform wars are going for the next several years.

This has led to a significant increase in short-term rental properties, often to the detriment of local residents. The situation has become so dire that authorities have taken drastic measures, including imposing massive fines on companies that flout the rules.

What Happened — The Full Story

The fine imposed on Airbnb is equal to six times the profits the company made between the time authorities warned the company and when the listings were taken down. The consumer affairs ministry found that over 65,000 Airbnb adverts breached Spanish consumer protection rules, including listing properties without licenses or with license numbers that did not match official registers. This is not an isolated incident, as Barcelona has become the most visible flashpoint, announcing plans to phase out all tourist apartments by 2028.

In June, Spain also ordered Booking.com to remove more than 4,000 illegal accommodation listings. The country's authorities have been cracking down on companies that exploit the housing shortage for profit. Consumer rights minister Pablo Bustinduy stated that there were 'thousands of families who are living on the edge' because of the housing crunch, while some companies profited from 'business models that expel people from their homes.' The situation is complex, with many factors at play, but the Spanish government is determined to address the issue.

The impact of the housing shortage is being felt across the country, with a record number of tourists visiting Spain and putting pressure on the housing market. Authorities have recently removed more than 53,000 illegal tourist flats from official registers nationwide, the bulk of which were located in Andalusia, the Canary Islands, Catalonia, and Valencia. The situation is dire, and the government is taking steps to mitigate the effects of the housing shortage.

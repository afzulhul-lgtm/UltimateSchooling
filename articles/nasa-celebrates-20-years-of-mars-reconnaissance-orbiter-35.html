NASA Celebrates 20 Years of Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter

When the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter (MRO) fired its main engine on March 10, 2006 and slipped into Mars orbit after a seven-month journey from Earth, the mission was designed to last two years. Twenty years later, MRO is still orbiting Mars every two hours, its cameras, radars, and spectrometers still running, still returning data, still teaching us things about the Red Planet that no instrument on Earth could discover.

In those two decades, MRO has transmitted more than 459 terabytes of data — more than all previous Mars missions combined. It has captured over 7.6 million images of the Martian surface. It has been the primary communication relay for every Mars surface mission since 2006. And it has fundamentally changed what we know about Mars — its geology, its history of water, its weather, and its potential as a destination for human exploration.

Mission Overview — What MRO Is and What It Was Built For

MRO was built by Lockheed Martin Space Systems and launched from Cape Canaveral on August 12, 2005 aboard an Atlas V rocket. Its primary mission objectives were: to study Mars's geological and climatological history in detail; to identify potential landing sites for future missions; and to determine whether Mars ever had water in quantities sufficient to support life. It was also required to serve as a high-bandwidth communications relay between Earth and future Mars landers.

The spacecraft cost approximately $720 million to build and launch. At over 2,180 kg fully fueled, it was the largest Mars orbiter ever built at the time of launch. It carries the most powerful collection of science instruments ever sent to another planet — six science instruments that together cover imaging, spectroscopy, atmospheric monitoring, radar sounding, and radiometry.

HiRISE: The Most Powerful Camera Ever Sent to Another Planet

The High Resolution Imaging Science Experiment (HiRISE) is the instrument that most people think of when they think of MRO. Built at the University of Arizona, it is a 0.5-meter aperture reflecting telescope — comparable in size to many ground-based astronomical telescopes — pointed at the surface of Mars from 300 km above.

From that altitude, HiRISE can resolve surface features as small as 25–30 centimeters — roughly the size of a dinner plate. No other camera in history has achieved this resolution from planetary orbit. The practical results are extraordinary: HiRISE has imaged avalanches in progress in real time, captured dust devils as they spun across the surface, photographed fresh impact craters as they appeared between successive overflights, documented the seasonal progression of CO2 ice patterns at the poles, and revealed fine-scale geological structures that would require a geologist on the surface to observe with naked eyes on Earth.

HiRISE has also photographed multiple Mars landers and rovers from orbit — including Curiosity and Perseverance — with enough detail to see the vehicles' tracks in the dust. Before each rover landing, HiRISE images of the target landing ellipse were used to assess surface hazards. The Phoenix lander was photographed actually descending on its parachute from orbit — one of the most iconic planetary science images ever taken.

The Water Story: MRO's Most Important Discovery

The single most scientifically significant contribution of MRO's 20 years is its comprehensive documentation of water's role in shaping Mars — past and present. Multiple instruments have contributed to this story.

CRISM confirmed the presence of phyllosilicate (clay) minerals across widespread areas of the ancient Martian highlands. Clay minerals can only form through prolonged contact with liquid water — their presence is direct mineralogical evidence that Mars had liquid water on its surface for extended periods billions of years ago. CRISM also detected sulfates, carbonates, and other water-associated minerals, building a detailed map of where and when water shaped the Martian crust.

HiRISE documented a class of features called Recurring Slope Lineae (RSL) — dark streaks that appear and grow on steep slopes during warmer Martian seasons, then fade. Initial observations suggested these could be caused by liquid briny water seeping from the subsurface. More recent analysis suggests they may instead be caused by dry granular flows, but the debate continues and MRO data remains central to it.

SHARAD confirmed extensive deposits of water ice buried beneath the Martian surface at the poles and at mid-latitudes — ice that is not just a thin frost but in some locations represents reservoirs of water potentially critical for future human missions.

SHARAD: What's Buried Beneath the Surface

The Shallow Radar (SHARAD) instrument represents a completely different way of seeing Mars. Rather than imaging the surface, SHARAD transmits radio waves downward and measures how they reflect off buried layers — the same principle as ground-penetrating radar used by archaeologists and geologists on Earth, but operating from 300 km above the planet.

SHARAD can see up to approximately 1 kilometer beneath the surface, with vertical resolution of around 15 meters. Its findings have been among MRO's most dramatic: confirming that the north polar cap contains layers of water ice and dust extending hundreds of meters deep; revealing buried impact craters filled with ice; and detecting ice-rich deposits at mid-latitudes that were not visible from the surface.

Why Subsurface Ice Matters for Human Exploration

Water ice buried under the Martian surface is one of the most critical resources for any future human mission to Mars. It can be mined, melted, and used for drinking water, for growing food, and — crucially — for splitting into hydrogen and oxygen to make rocket propellant for a return journey to Earth. MRO's mapping of subsurface ice locations is directly guiding where human landing sites may eventually be chosen.

CRISM: Reading Mars's Mineral Record

While HiRISE shows what Mars looks like, CRISM shows what Mars is made of. By measuring light reflected from the surface in 544 different wavelength channels spanning visible and near-infrared light, CRISM can identify specific minerals by their spectral fingerprints — in the same way that a forensics lab can identify unknown substances by their spectral signature.

CRISM's mineral map of Mars has become one of the foundational datasets in planetary science. It has identified over 200 distinct mineralogical environments on Mars, telling a story of a planet that went through distinct geological and climatic eras. The early wet era produced phyllosilicates and carbonates. A later acidic era produced sulfates. The current frozen, arid era maintains the iron oxides that give Mars its red color. Together, CRISM's data reveals a complete geological biography of the planet.

MRO as a Communication Relay

MRO's science instruments are its headline contribution, but its operational role as a communication relay has been equally important. Mars rovers and landers communicate with Earth via UHF radio to MRO and other orbiters overhead, which then relay those signals to Earth using far more powerful X-band and Ka-band transmitters.

Without MRO as a relay, data from surface missions would be transmitted at a fraction of the rate, dramatically limiting science return. MRO has served as the primary relay for the Phoenix lander, the Curiosity rover, and is currently the main relay for the Perseverance rover and its associated Ingenuity helicopter. Every science image and data packet from Perseverance's ongoing mission reaches Earth through MRO.

What Comes Next

MRO is still operational in 2026, but NASA has flagged concerns about its aging hardware. The spacecraft's reaction control thrusters — used for orbital adjustments and attitude control — have been used far more than their original design specifications allowed. The team manages the propellant budget carefully, and some maneuver capabilities have been limited as a result.

NASA's current planning assumes MRO will continue to function through at least the late 2020s, where it will play a critical role supporting the planned Mars Sample Return mission — the effort to retrieve rock and soil samples collected by the Perseverance rover and return them to Earth. MRO will serve as a communications relay for the Earth Return Orbiter and lander that will collect Perseverance's cached samples.

After MRO, NASA's Mars orbital science capability will eventually need a successor. The agency has discussed a future Mars Environmental Dynamics Analyzer Orbiter or equivalent next-generation mission, but as of 2026 no funded successor to MRO's science capabilities has been formally approved.

  • MRO launched August 12, 2005; arrived at Mars March 10, 2006. Originally designed for 2 years, it is now in its 20th year of Mars orbit.
  • Has transmitted 459+ terabytes of data — more than all other Mars missions combined — and captured 7.6+ million images.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter (MRO)?

MRO is a NASA spacecraft that has been orbiting Mars since March 10, 2006 — now 20 years. Originally designed for a 2-year mission, it carries six science instruments and has sent back more data than all previous Mars missions combined.

How much data has the MRO sent back?

As of 2026, MRO has transmitted more than 459 terabytes of data and captured over 7.6 million images of Mars.

What is HiRISE and what has it found?

HiRISE is a 0.5-meter telescope-camera that resolves surface features as small as 25–30 cm from orbit. It has imaged avalanches in progress, fresh impact craters forming, dust devils, Mars rovers and landers, and seasonal ice patterns — the highest-resolution images ever taken of any planet's surface from orbit.

Has MRO found evidence of water on Mars?

Yes. CRISM detected clay minerals requiring ancient liquid water. SHARAD confirmed water ice buried beneath the surface. HiRISE documented Recurring Slope Lineae that may involve liquid brine. Together these represent the most comprehensive evidence ever assembled for Mars's water history.

What is SHARAD and what did it discover?

SHARAD is a ground-penetrating radar that sees up to 1km beneath the Martian surface. It confirmed extensive water ice deposits at the poles and mid-latitudes, and buried geological structures not visible from the surface.

Is MRO still operational in 2026?

Yes, fully operational. NASA has flagged aging thrusters as a concern, but the spacecraft continues to function and will support Mars Sample Return mission operations through the late 2020s.

Conclusion

The Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter's 20-year anniversary is not just a celebration — it is a reminder of what sustained, patient scientific investment looks like. A spacecraft designed for two years, built with the best instruments available in 2005, is still generating new scientific discoveries in 2026. The 459 terabytes of data it has collected represent an archive that scientists will continue mining for decades.

MRO transformed Mars from a planet we vaguely understood into one we have mapped in extraordinary detail — its mineralogy, its buried ice, its weather, its geological history, and the precise locations where future humans might one day land. The water story it told — from ancient liquid oceans to buried ice to possible modern seeps — changed the fundamental scientific question from "Was Mars ever habitable?" to "Could it still be, and where?"

When humans finally set foot on Mars, they will step onto a planet that MRO spent 20 years preparing for their arrival.

Variable Aperture: What It Is and Why Samsung Dropped It

Variable aperture is one of the most requested camera features in smartphone photography — and one of the most complicated to implement well. The concept is simple: instead of a fixed lens opening, the camera physically adjusts the size of the aperture depending on the scene. A wider opening (lower f-number like f/1.4) lets in more light and creates shallow depth-of-field blur. A narrower opening (higher f-number like f/4.0) keeps the entire frame sharp and prevents bright areas from overexposing.

Samsung was actually the first major brand to offer variable aperture on a smartphone, debuting it on the Galaxy S9 in 2018 — a system that physically switched between f/1.5 and f/2.4. It continued with the Galaxy S10, then quietly dropped the feature as Samsung shifted focus to the megapixel race. The feature was later adopted by brands including OPPO, Xiaomi, HONOR, and Huawei, while Samsung sat it out for several years.

The revival for the S27 Ultra would be more ambitious than the S9/S10 implementation. Instead of two fixed positions, the rumored f/1.4 to f/4.0 range suggests a continuously adjustable aperture — closer to what you find on a dedicated camera lens. Paired with a 200MP sensor, this would give users genuine photographic control that no software simulation can replicate.

LOFIC Technology Explained

Of the two major camera upgrades rumored for the S27 Ultra, LOFIC (Lateral Overflow Integration Capacitor) may actually be the more impactful one — even if its name is less headline-friendly than variable aperture.

Here's how it works: every image sensor pixel has a maximum capacity for storing electrical charge from incoming light — called the full-well capacity. In a high-contrast scene (think a bright window against a dark room), the pixels exposed to the bright areas fill up and overflow. That overflow is normally discarded, causing bright areas to appear as pure white with no detail — what photographers call "blown highlights."

LOFIC fixes this by pairing each pixel with a dedicated overflow capacitor. Instead of discarding the excess charge, the capacitor captures it. The image processing pipeline then reads data from both the pixel and the capacitor, combining them into a single exposure with a dramatically wider dynamic range. No HDR stacking, no multi-frame blending — just a single, clean shot with detail preserved in both shadows and highlights.

The Xiaomi 17 Ultra already uses LOFIC on its primary camera and has been praised for its natural HDR performance. If Samsung implements the same technology on the ISOCELL HP6/HPA, the S27 Ultra could finally close the dynamic range gap that has consistently favored Google's Pixel and Apple's iPhone in side-by-side camera comparisons.

The New Triple Camera Setup

One of the more controversial elements of the S27 Ultra leak is the reported switch from four cameras to three. Every Galaxy S Ultra since the S22 Ultra has included a dedicated 10MP 3x telephoto lens. The S27 Ultra is reportedly dropping it entirely, leaving a triple array of the 200MP main, 50MP ultrawide, and 50MP 5x periscope telephoto.

The rationale makes engineering sense. The 200MP primary sensor captures an enormous amount of detail, making high-quality crop-based zoom at 2x and 3x entirely feasible without a dedicated lens. Removing the 3x telephoto frees up both physical space inside the phone's camera module and engineering budget — both of which Samsung appears to be redirecting toward the new sensor hardware and the mechanical variable aperture mechanism.

This strategy also aligns with a broader industry trend. Apple has been moving toward fewer, larger sensors rather than cramming in extra lenses. Google's Pixel 9 series similarly relies on computational zoom from high-resolution sensors. Whether Samsung's execution will match the convenience of having a dedicated 3x lens remains to be seen, but the tradeoff appears to be a deliberate choice rather than a cost-cutting measure.

Should You Wait for the Galaxy S27 Ultra?

If smartphone photography is your main priority, the leaked S27 Ultra specs make a strong case for patience. The combination of variable aperture, LOFIC dynamic range, and a higher-resolution 1/1.12-inch sensor represents a genuine hardware step forward — not the incremental refresh that the Galaxy S26 Ultra turned out to be.

However, the S27 Ultra is still approximately 8–9 months away. If your current phone is failing you today, the Galaxy S26 Ultra and iPhone 16 Pro both remain excellent cameras right now. The decision to wait should also factor in: the S27 Ultra's expected price (likely $1,299+), unconfirmed final specs, and whether the dropped 3x lens is something you'd miss in your daily workflow.

For Android users specifically, the S27 Ultra stands out because no current Samsung flagship offers LOFIC — giving it a meaningful technical edge over everything in Samsung's current lineup the moment it ships.

Frequently Asked Questions

What camera upgrades is the Galaxy S27 Ultra getting?

According to leaks from Weibo tipster SmartPikachu, the S27 Ultra will feature a 200MP ISOCELL HP6 (or HPA variant) sensor with mechanical variable aperture (likely f/1.4 to f/4.0) and LOFIC technology. The camera array is also reportedly switching to a triple-camera setup — dropping the 3x telephoto — with a 50MP ultrawide and 50MP 5x periscope lens completing the system.

What is LOFIC technology and why does it matter?

LOFIC (Lateral Overflow Integration Capacitor) pairs each pixel with a dedicated capacitor that captures overflow light from bright areas. This produces dramatically wider dynamic range in a single exposure — no multi-frame HDR stacking required. The Xiaomi 17 Ultra already uses it and has received strong reviews for natural HDR output.

What is variable aperture and how does it help photography?

Variable aperture lets the lens mechanically adjust its opening. Wide aperture (f/1.4) captures more light and creates background blur, ideal for low-light and portrait shots. Narrow aperture (f/4.0) sharpens the whole frame and prevents overexposure in bright conditions. Samsung last offered this on the Galaxy S9 and S10.

Is Samsung dropping the 3x telephoto on the S27 Ultra?

Yes, according to current leaks. The S27 Ultra is reportedly moving to a triple-camera system, relying on the 200MP primary sensor's crop-based zoom for 2x and 3x focal lengths instead of a dedicated lens.

When will the Samsung Galaxy S27 Ultra be released?

The Galaxy S27 Ultra is expected to launch in early 2027, consistent with Samsung's typical Galaxy S Unpacked schedule. No official announcement has been made.

Is Samsung reviving variable aperture because of Apple?

Possibly. Reports suggest the iPhone 18 Pro will also feature variable aperture — and Samsung was actually the original pioneer with the Galaxy S9 in 2018. The timing of Samsung's revival, coinciding with Apple's rumored adoption, has led many analysts to suggest competitive pressure is a factor, though the feature has genuine photographic merit regardless.

Conclusion

The Galaxy S27 Ultra camera leak paints a picture of Samsung finally taking smartphone camera hardware seriously again. After years of iterating on the same 200MP sensor with incremental aperture tweaks, the combination of mechanical variable aperture and LOFIC single-exposure HDR represents the kind of meaningful hardware upgrade that enthusiasts have been waiting for.

Samsung invented variable aperture, abandoned it, and now appears ready to bring it back in a more capable form than before. Whether the execution lives up to the leaked specs — and whether dropping the 3x telephoto turns out to be a wise trade-off — will be the defining questions when the S27 Ultra launches in early 2027.

For now, the early leaks are encouraging. If Samsung delivers on even half of what's been rumored, the Galaxy S27 Ultra could genuinely challenge Apple for the mobile photography crown in 2027's flagship season.

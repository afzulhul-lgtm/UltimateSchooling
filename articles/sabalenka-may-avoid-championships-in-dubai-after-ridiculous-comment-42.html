Sabalenka May Avoid Championships in Dubai After 'Ridiculous' Comment

World No.1 Aryna Sabalenka has threatened to stay away from the Dubai Tennis Championships permanently after tournament director Salah Tahlak publicly questioned her withdrawal from the 2026 event and called for harsher penalties for top players who pull out of tournaments. Sabalenka responded at a media day at the Miami Open on March 17, 2026 — her first opportunity to address Tahlak's remarks after going silent at Indian Wells.

The spat is more than a player-versus-organizer squabble. It has ignited a long-simmering debate about the WTA's grueling schedule, the obligations placed on top players under mandatory event rules, and whether tournament directors have the right to publicly question medical and scheduling decisions made by athletes.

Sabalenka's Exact Words at Miami Open

Speaking to reporters at the Miami Open media day, Sabalenka held nothing back. Her full statement on Tahlak's comments:

"I think it's ridiculous. I don't think he showed himself in the best way possible. For me it's actually so sad to see that the tournament directors and the tournaments not protecting us as players. They just care about their sales, about their tournament and that's it. His comment was ridiculous. I'm not sure if I ever want to go there after his comment. For me it's too much." — Aryna Sabalenka, Miami Open media day, March 17, 2026 (via Sky Sports)

Sabalenka went further when asked about her overall approach to the 2026 season and why she had skipped both Dubai and Doha:

"Going into this season, we decided to prioritise my health and make sure we have these little gaps in the schedule where I can reset, recharge, work and be better prepared for bigger tournaments. I feel like the scheduling is going crazy and that's why you see so many players injured, always taped and not delivering the best quality matches because it's almost impossible." — Aryna Sabalenka, Miami Open, March 17, 2026 (via Sky Sports)

What Dubai Director Salah Tahlak Actually Said

Following the withdrawals of both Sabalenka and Swiatek on February 14, 2026 — one day before the tournament began — Tahlak gave an interview to The National in which he made several pointed comments:

"It was an unfortunate surprise to get news of the withdrawal of Aryna and Iga. And the reasons for withdrawal were a bit strange. Iga said she wasn't mentally ready to compete, while Sabalenka said she has some minor injuries. I don't know. I think there should be a harsher punishment on the players — not just fines; they should be docked ranking points. I even asked the tournament doctor, what is the injury? He said it's a minor injury, not one that would force her to withdraw from the tournament. And for Iga, I asked, 'Isn't this a strange decision?'" — Salah Tahlak, Dubai tournament director, February 2026 (via The National)

The comments drew immediate criticism from the tennis community. The suggestion that Tahlak consulted the tournament's own doctor to evaluate Sabalenka's injury — rather than trusting the player's own medical team — was particularly controversial. Consulting a tournament-affiliated physician to challenge a player's withdrawal is a striking step, and Sabalenka's response that he "crossed the line" reflects how personally she took it.

Sabalenka's withdrawal was not isolated. She also skipped the Doha WTA 1000 event the same week, confirming that her team made a deliberate pre-season decision to leave gaps in her schedule rather than compete in back-to-back mandatory events at the start of the year. This is a recognized and increasingly common strategy among top players who have found the WTA's mandatory event system physically unsustainable over a full season.

Indian Wells: The Withdrawal Vindicated

There is a satisfying postscript to Tahlak's suggestion that Sabalenka's hip injury wasn't serious enough to justify missing Dubai. Three weeks later, Sabalenka won the Indian Wells Masters title for the first time in her career — defeating Elena Rybakina in an intense final that she described as "a crazy match."

Speaking about the victory, Sabalenka directly referenced the Miami Open she was now entering: "I'm defending champion there, so I have to bring my best tennis there. But with these vibes that I'm taking from here, I think I'll be able to be there and to fight and to do my very best to defend that beautiful trophy."

The sequence — skip Dubai and Doha, win Indian Wells — is a direct validation of the "prioritize health" strategy that Tahlak publicly questioned. Players and their medical teams, not tournament doctors or directors, are in the best position to evaluate individual readiness.

The Bigger Picture: WTA Scheduling and Player Welfare

Sabalenka's broader comments about the schedule are not a new complaint — they are part of a years-long conversation in professional tennis about the mandatory event system and the physical toll of the tour calendar. Under WTA rules, top players are required to compete in all four Grand Slams, 10 WTA 1000 events, and six WTA 500 events. Penalties for missing events include ranking point deductions and fines.

WTA Mandatory Event Rules

The Dubai Tennis Championships is a WTA 1000 event — one of the ten mandatory stops that top-ranked players must enter each year. Players ranked in the top 8 who withdraw late face both fine penalties and ranking point adjustments. Tahlak's call for harsher penalties would extend the existing system — a proposal Sabalenka argues incentivizes players to play through injuries rather than protect their long-term health.

Sabalenka's argument — that the schedule is "going crazy" and produces a constant stream of taped-up, injury-compromised players delivering below-peak matches — is not a personal grievance. It is a structural critique that multiple top players, including Novak Djokovic on the ATP side and Iga Swiatek on the WTA, have made in recent years. The difference is that Sabalenka said it directly in response to a named individual who had publicly called her out.

What It Means for Dubai if Sabalenka Stays Away

The Dubai Tennis Championships is one of the most prestigious events in the WTA 1000 calendar — drawing strong fields, major sponsors, and significant television viewership. Sabalenka, as the world No.1 and one of the most marketable players on tour, is a significant commercial draw for any event she enters.

Sabalenka's threat to skip Dubai is not unprecedented in tennis. Player-tournament feuds have occasionally led to extended absences from specific events. But a world No.1 publicly declaring she may never return to a WTA 1000 is a high-profile statement with real commercial implications for the tournament. It is also a situation the WTA will need to manage — Tahlak's public comments about Sabalenka's medical decisions crossed a line that could attract scrutiny from the WTA's own player relations processes.

Frequently Asked Questions

What exactly did Sabalenka say about the Dubai tournament director?

At the Miami Open on March 17, 2026, Sabalenka called Tahlak's comments "ridiculous" and "shameful," said the tournament was only interested in its own sales rather than protecting players, and stated: "I'm not sure if I ever want to go there after his comment. For me it's too much."

Why did Sabalenka withdraw from Dubai?

Sabalenka announced her withdrawal on February 14, 2026 — one day before the tournament — citing a minor right hip injury. She also skipped Doha as part of a deliberate decision to create recovery gaps in her 2026 schedule.

What did Salah Tahlak say about Sabalenka's withdrawal?

Tahlak called the withdrawal an "unfortunate surprise," described the reasons as "a bit strange," consulted the tournament doctor who said Sabalenka's injury was "minor and not one that would force her to withdraw," and called for ranking point deductions as punishment for last-minute withdrawals.

Is Dubai a mandatory WTA event?

Yes. Dubai is a WTA 1000 event. Under WTA rules, top-ranked players must enter all 10 WTA 1000 events and face penalties including point deductions and fines for late withdrawals. Tahlak argued the existing penalties weren't severe enough.

What happened after Sabalenka withdrew from Dubai?

Sabalenka returned at Indian Wells and won the title for the first time in her career, defeating Elena Rybakina in the final. She then competed at the Miami Open as the defending champion.

Who else withdrew from the Dubai tournament?

World No.3 Iga Swiatek also withdrew on February 14, citing a "change of schedule." Tahlak questioned Swiatek's reasons as well, calling her decision "strange."

Conclusion

The Sabalenka-Tahlak dispute is, at its core, about who has the right to determine when a player is fit to compete. Tahlak's decision to consult the tournament's own doctor to evaluate Sabalenka's injury — and then publicize that evaluation — was a significant overreach that Sabalenka's reaction reflects accurately. Medical decisions belong to athletes and their own medical teams, not to tournament directors with commercial interests in those athletes' participation.

Sabalenka's Indian Wells title immediately after the Dubai withdrawal makes the "she didn't need to pull out" argument look thin. A player returning from a recovery gap to win one of the biggest non-Slam events on the calendar is, by any measure, evidence that prioritizing health over an early-season WTA 1000 was the right call.

Whether Sabalenka follows through on her threat to avoid Dubai permanently remains to be seen. The WTA has financial and structural incentives to keep its top players participating in all mandatory events. But Tahlak's public statements have created a rift that will not be easily mended, and the broader scheduling debate Sabalenka opened — one that resonates across the locker room — is not going away.

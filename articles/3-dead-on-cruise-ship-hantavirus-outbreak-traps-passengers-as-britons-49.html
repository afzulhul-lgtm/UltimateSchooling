3 Dead on Cruise Ship: Hantavirus Outbreak Traps Passengers as Britons Self-Isolate After Leaving Early

A Dutch-flagged polar expedition cruise ship, the MV Hondius, became the center of an international health emergency after a hantavirus outbreak killed three passengers and infected at least eleven people across multiple nationalities. The ship — operated by Oceanwide Expeditions and carrying passengers and crew from 23 countries — was quarantined at sea for weeks before docking in the Canary Islands on May 10, 2026 for a carefully coordinated 22-country evacuation.

WHO Director-General Dr. Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus personally traveled to Tenerife to oversee the operation, reassuring the public: "This is not another COVID. And the risk to the public is low. So they shouldn't be scared, and they shouldn't panic."

What Happened on the MV Hondius

The MV Hondius departed Argentina in early April 2026 on a polar expedition cruise. The ship carries passengers on remote voyages to destinations such as Antarctica, South Georgia, and the South Atlantic islands. On April 24, the vessel made a scheduled stop at St Helena — the remote British island territory in the South Atlantic — where 30 passengers disembarked, including the body of the first victim and his wife, who were both airlifted to Johannesburg for medical treatment.

The first victim's wife died in a Johannesburg hospital on April 26. The outbreak was not publicly reported until May 2, when the World Health Organization (WHO) first received a formal notification. By that point, additional passengers were showing symptoms, and the ship had sailed north toward Cape Verde.

On May 3, the ship docked at Praia, the capital of Cape Verde, but local authorities were unable to manage a safe evacuation given the scale of the outbreak and the number of nationalities involved. The ship departed Cape Verde on May 6, bound for the Canary Islands under Spanish authorization.

The Virus: Andes Hantavirus Confirmed

By May 4, gene sequencing had identified the Andes virus (ANDV) in at least one infected person. A full isolate sequence from a Swiss resident was published on the scientific platform virological.org on May 8, designated ANDV/Switzerland/Hu-3337/2026.

The Andes virus is critically important in the context of this outbreak because it is the only known hantavirus strain with documented human-to-human transmission. All other hantavirus strains are transmitted exclusively through contact with infected rodents — their urine, droppings, or saliva. The Andes virus, however, can spread between people through close, sustained contact and is possibly airborne in some circumstances.

This distinction is why the outbreak attracted such intense international attention. Standard hantavirus outbreaks are not contagious between people. The Andes strain changes that risk profile — though health authorities consistently emphasized that transmission still requires prolonged close contact, and the risk of a wider epidemic remains low based on historical patterns.

One of the evacuated passengers was a 56-year-old British man, who was initially and incorrectly identified as the ship's doctor by Spain's health minister. The ship's actual doctor — Dr. Steve Kornfeld, a physician who stepped in after the ship's own medic fell ill — later tested positive himself and was placed in the biocontainment unit at the University of Nebraska Medical Center. Speaking to CNN from quarantine, Kornfeld said he was experiencing no current symptoms and was in good spirits, spending time on WhatsApp.

The Two Britons Who Left the Ship Early

Among those who disembarked at St Helena on April 24 were two British nationals who flew home via Johannesburg before the outbreak was publicly identified. After news of the hantavirus outbreak broke in early May, they contacted UK health officials themselves. The UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) confirmed that both individuals were subsequently confirmed as infected with hantavirus — they are among five passengers who left the ship early and later tested positive.

A total of 30 passengers disembarked at St Helena, all of whom have been contact-traced by the UKHSA. Foreign Secretary Yvette Cooper described the situation as "very serious and deeply stressful for those affected and their families," confirming that consular staff were in direct contact with British nationals both on the ship and in their home countries.

Those exposed face a self-isolation or monitoring period of up to six weeks from the date of their last potential contact, given the Andes virus's longer incubation window compared to standard hantavirus strains.

The Tenerife Evacuation: How It Unfolded

The MV Hondius docked at the Port of Granadilla in Tenerife on May 10. The evacuation was one of the most logistically complex international health operations in recent memory — 22 countries coordinating simultaneously to repatriate their nationals in a medically safe manner.

Spanish passengers were the first to disembark. They were transported directly to a military hospital in Madrid. Hours later, French evacuees were flown home — though one passenger developed symptoms en route, triggering isolation protocols for all five people on that flight upon landing. American passengers — 17 citizens plus one British US resident — were flown to Offutt Air Force Base in Omaha, Nebraska, then transferred to the University of Nebraska Medical Center's National Quarantine Unit, a federally funded biocontainment facility. Fifteen were placed under monitoring; one, who tested positive, was in the biocontainment unit.

Passengers could be seen wearing full-body blue protective suits and breathing masks as they transferred by smaller boats to shore. Workers at the port wore hazmat suits, face masks, and respirators throughout the operation. Video footage showed passengers on the tarmac being sprayed with disinfectant before boarding their repatriation flights.

British Army Parachutes Into Tristan da Cunha

One of the most extraordinary episodes of this outbreak unfolded on Tristan da Cunha — Britain's most remote inhabited overseas territory, located roughly 2,400 kilometers from the nearest inhabited island. One of the island's 221 residents — a passenger who had disembarked from the MV Hondius the previous month — developed a suspected case of hantavirus.

Because Tristan da Cunha has no airstrip and is normally only reachable by a six-day boat voyage from Cape Town, the UK Ministry of Defence deployed a team of six paratroopers and two medical clinicians, who parachuted from a Royal Air Force transport plane. The aircraft also dropped oxygen supplies and medical equipment. It was a rare peacetime use of military medical parachuting capability.

What Is Hantavirus and How Dangerous Is It?

Hantaviruses are a group of more than 50 virus types that naturally infect rodents — particularly mice and rats — worldwide. Humans typically become infected by breathing in dust or aerosols contaminated with infected rodent urine, droppings, or saliva, or through direct contact with these materials via cuts or the eyes.

Symptoms typically appear within one to eight weeks of exposure and progress in two phases:

Phase Symptoms Duration
Early (Prodromal) Fever, extreme fatigue, muscle aches, headache, stomach pain, nausea, vomiting 3–5 days
Severe (Cardiopulmonary) Rapid breathing, fluid in lungs, low blood pressure, lung damage, internal bleeding, kidney failure Days to weeks

The Andes virus specifically causes Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome (HPS), which has a case fatality rate historically ranging from 35% to 50% in untreated cases. There is currently no specific antiviral drug approved for hantavirus infection; treatment focuses on intensive supportive care, including mechanical ventilation in severe cases.

Key Difference: Andes Virus vs. Other Hantaviruses

All other hantavirus strains only spread from infected rodents to humans. The Andes virus is uniquely dangerous because it is the only hantavirus confirmed to spread between people — though this still requires close, prolonged contact, such as sleeping in the same household. Casual contact or proximity on a large ship is considered very low risk.

What the WHO and CDC Are Saying

The international health response to this outbreak was notably swift. The WHO deployed a field team to Tenerife led by Dr. Maria Van Kerkhove, who noted: "This is what WHO does — the agency routinely coordinates international responses to infectious disease threats even when public attention is limited."

The CDC classified the MV Hondius outbreak as a Level 3 emergency response — its highest emergency classification — and deployed a team of epidemiologists and medical professionals to the Canary Islands. The CDC also developed specific health guidance for American passengers, distributed via the US Department of State.

The ECDC stated that the risk to the EU/EEA general population is very low, emphasizing that the Andes virus requires close, sustained contact for person-to-person transmission, and that previous outbreaks have only involved transmission in close-contact household settings.

Conclusion

The MV Hondius hantavirus outbreak has been a genuine international health emergency — one that tested the coordination capacity of 22 countries, the WHO, the CDC, and the ECDC simultaneously. Three people lost their lives to the Andes virus, a rare and particularly dangerous strain that added human-to-human transmission risk to an already deadly disease.

The evacuation operation in Tenerife was a logistical achievement: repatriating passengers of 19 nationalities in a single day, with military-grade protective protocols and no confirmed transmission events during the disembarkation process. The British Army parachuting medics onto Tristan da Cunha served as a striking reminder of how far the human consequences of a shipboard outbreak can reach.

With all passengers now repatriated and under monitoring in their home countries, the immediate crisis has passed. But health authorities across Europe and North America will continue tracking this cohort for up to six weeks — the outer limit of the Andes virus's known incubation period. Passengers or contacts who develop fever, severe fatigue, or muscle aches during this window should contact their local health authority immediately and mention their connection to the MV Hondius.

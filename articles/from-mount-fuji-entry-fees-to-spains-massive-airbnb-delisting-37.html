A record 94 million foreign tourists visited Spain in 2024, and 2026 is on track to break that record again. But behind the headline numbers lies a deepening crisis: the flood of short-term rental properties has pushed housing costs beyond the reach of ordinary Spanish residents, and the government has finally decided it has had enough. Spain's consumer affairs ministry has handed Airbnb a €65 million fine — one of the largest penalties ever imposed on a short-term rental platform — for advertising over 65,000 properties that were either banned, unlicensed, or fraudulently listed. At the same time, Japan is charging tourists to climb Mount Fuji, and cities from Amsterdam to Dubrovnik are imposing new limits on visitor numbers. The era of unchecked mass tourism is coming to an end.

Spain Fines Airbnb €65 Million — What Happened

Spain's consumer affairs ministry issued the €65 million penalty after finding that Airbnb had continued to advertise properties in clear violation of Spanish law — even after being formally warned. The investigation revealed three categories of illegal listings: properties that had been explicitly banned from operating as short-term rentals, properties being advertised without any license whatsoever, and properties using license numbers that did not match official government registers.

Consumer rights minister Pablo Bustinduy was blunt in his assessment of the situation. He stated publicly that there were thousands of families living on the edge of their financial limits because of the housing crunch, while certain companies were profiting from business models that effectively expelled people from their own homes. His framing of the fine was explicitly moral as well as legal: this was not just a regulatory violation, it was a social harm.

Airbnb, for its part, has contested the fine and is expected to appeal. The company has previously argued that it acts as a platform and that responsibility for compliance lies with individual hosts rather than with Airbnb itself — an argument that Spanish regulators have now explicitly and expensively rejected.

How the €65 Million Fine Was Calculated

The methodology behind the fine is significant, as it sets a precedent for how regulators may approach platform liability in future cases. Spanish authorities did not simply impose a flat penalty — they calculated the fine as six times the profits Airbnb generated from the illegal listings during the period between the official government warning and the eventual removal of those listings.

This approach is deliberately designed to eliminate the financial incentive for non-compliance. If a company can simply continue profiting from illegal activity while a fine is being calculated based on a fixed rate, the rational business decision may be to keep the listings up and pay the fine later. By tying the penalty directly to profits multiplied by a factor of six, Spanish authorities have made non-compliance economically irrational — even for a company of Airbnb's size.

Regulatory Precedent

Spain's profit-multiplier approach to calculating the Airbnb fine could become a template for other EU member states cracking down on short-term rental platforms. Legal analysts say the method is specifically designed to remove the financial logic of continued non-compliance.

Barcelona's Plan to Ban All Tourist Apartments by 2028

Barcelona has become the most prominent flashpoint in Spain's battle against short-term rentals. The city has announced plans to phase out every single tourist apartment within its boundaries by 2028 — a move with no direct equivalent anywhere else in Europe. When existing short-term rental licenses expire, they will not be renewed. New licenses will not be issued. The city's approximately 10,000 tourist apartments will, in theory, cease to exist as a legal category.

The decision reflects years of mounting pressure from residents, particularly in the city's most desirable neighborhoods. In districts like the Gothic Quarter, Barceloneta, and Eixample, the conversion of residential flats into tourist rentals has hollowed out communities, driven up rents for those who remain, and turned formerly residential streets into something closer to hotel corridors. Residents have organized protests, covered buildings in anti-tourism graffiti, and repeatedly called on the city government to act.

The 2028 ban does not affect hotels or other registered tourist accommodations — it is specifically targeted at the apartment-style short-term rentals that have proliferated via platforms like Airbnb. Property owners who currently hold licenses will be able to operate until those licenses expire, at which point they will need to convert their properties back to residential use or other commercial purposes.

Booking.com Also Ordered to Remove Illegal Listings

Airbnb is not the only platform in the Spanish government's crosshairs. In a separate action, Spain ordered Booking.com to remove more than 4,000 illegal accommodation listings from its platform. The action follows a pattern: Spanish regulators have been systematically working through the major short-term rental platforms and demanding compliance with the country's licensing requirements.

The government has also removed over 53,000 illegal tourist flats from official registers nationwide — a figure that underscores just how widespread the problem had become. The bulk of these removals occurred in four regions: Andalusia (which includes Málaga, Seville, and the Costa del Sol), the Canary Islands, Catalonia (which includes Barcelona), and Valencia (which includes the city of Valencia and Benidorm).

Spain's Housing Crisis: The Root Cause

To understand why Spain has taken such aggressive action against short-term rental platforms, it is essential to understand the scale of the housing crisis driving the government's response. Spain is not acting out of anti-business sentiment — it is responding to a genuine social emergency that has been building for years.

The conversion of residential housing to short-term tourist rentals removes properties from the long-term rental market permanently — at least as long as the economics of tourist rentals remain more attractive than residential leases. In cities like Barcelona, Málaga, and San Sebastián, the supply of affordable long-term rental housing has shrunk dramatically over the past decade, while demand from both residents and newcomers has continued to grow. The result is a housing market that has become inaccessible for teachers, nurses, local government workers, and others on average wages.

For Travelers Planning to Visit Spain

If you are planning to book short-term accommodation in Spain via Airbnb or Booking.com, verify that your listing has a valid, verifiable license number. Barcelona will not issue new tourist apartment licenses, and staying in an unlicensed property could result in your booking being cancelled at short notice.

Mount Fuji Entry Fees: Japan's Overtourism Fight

Spain is not alone in taking dramatic action to manage the consequences of mass tourism. In Japan, authorities introduced a formal entry fee for climbers on the Yoshida Trail — the most popular of the four official routes up Mount Fuji — alongside a cap on the number of climbers allowed per day. The measures were introduced after years of complaints about litter, damage to vegetation, safety incidents caused by ill-prepared climbers, and the sheer visual blight of enormous queues snaking up one of the world's most iconic mountains.

The Yoshida Trail climber cap limits daily ascents and requires payment of an entry fee — with proceeds directed toward trail maintenance, environmental protection, and safety services. A section of the trail at the fifth station was also fitted with a barrier that is lowered during unsafe conditions or when the daily cap has been reached, physically preventing additional climbers from continuing upward.

The measures reflect a broader shift in Japan's approach to inbound tourism. After years of aggressively marketing Japan as a tourist destination, the country is now grappling with the social and environmental costs of its own success. Mount Fuji, Kyoto's Gion district, and popular neighborhoods in Tokyo and Osaka are all experiencing pressure from visitor volumes that local residents increasingly find intolerable.

What Travelers Need to Know Before Visiting Spain

For tourists planning trips to Spain, the regulatory crackdown on short-term rentals has practical implications. Here is what travelers should be aware of:

  • Check your listing's license: Any Airbnb or Booking.com property in Spain should display a valid, government-registered license number. If the number cannot be verified or does not appear in official registers, the listing may be illegal and subject to removal.
  • Barcelona bookings require extra care: With the city phasing out all tourist apartment licenses by 2028, the supply of legal short-term rentals in Barcelona will shrink significantly over the coming years. Existing bookings may be affected if a host's license expires before the stay date.
  • Hotels remain unaffected: The crackdown targets apartment-style short-term rentals specifically. Licensed hotels, hostels, and boutique accommodations continue to operate normally and are not subject to the new restrictions.
  • Cancellation risk is real: If you book through a platform and the underlying listing is found to be illegal, the platform may be required to remove it — potentially leaving you without accommodation, even close to your travel date.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Why did Spain fine Airbnb €65 million?

A: Spain's consumer affairs ministry fined Airbnb €65 million for advertising over 65,000 short-term rental properties that were either banned, lacked licenses, or had fraudulent license numbers — all in breach of Spanish consumer protection law. The fine was calculated at six times the profits Airbnb made from those listings during the non-compliance period.

Q: How many illegal Airbnb listings were found in Spain?

A: Over 65,000 Airbnb listings were found to be in violation of Spanish consumer protection rules. Spain has also removed more than 53,000 illegal tourist flats from official registers nationally, mainly in Andalusia, the Canary Islands, Catalonia, and Valencia.

Q: Is Barcelona banning all tourist apartments?

A: Yes. Barcelona has announced a plan to eliminate all tourist apartments by 2028. Existing licenses will not be renewed when they expire, and no new licenses will be issued. The ban applies specifically to apartment-style short-term rentals listed on platforms like Airbnb — hotels are not affected.

Q: Was Booking.com also fined in Spain?

A: Spain ordered Booking.com to remove more than 4,000 illegal accommodation listings as part of the broader crackdown on short-term rental platforms operating outside the country's licensing framework.

Q: What is the Mount Fuji entry fee for tourists?

A: Japan introduced an entry fee and a daily climber cap on the Yoshida Trail — the most popular route up Mount Fuji — to combat overtourism damage. A physical barrier was also installed at the fifth station to prevent additional climbers from ascending once the daily cap is reached.

Q: What is overtourism and why are countries cracking down on it?

A: Overtourism occurs when visitor numbers exceed what a destination's infrastructure, environment, and community can sustainably support. Countries are taking action because mass tourism is driving up housing costs, displacing residents, damaging heritage sites, and overwhelming public services — problems that have become especially acute as global travel volumes continue to set records year after year.

Conclusion

Spain's €65 million Airbnb fine, Barcelona's planned 2028 tourist apartment ban, Japan's Mount Fuji entry fees, and the wave of overtourism restrictions spreading across Europe represent something more than individual policy decisions. They are symptoms of a fundamental reckoning with the real costs of mass tourism — costs that have, for decades, been borne almost entirely by local residents rather than the visitors, platforms, and property owners who profit from the industry.

The Spanish government's decision to fine Airbnb at six times its profits — rather than a fixed regulatory penalty — sends a clear message: the era of platforms treating consumer protection laws as an acceptable business risk is over. Combined with Barcelona's 2028 ban, the removal of 53,000 illegal flats from national registers, and the parallel action against Booking.com, Spain has signaled that it is prepared to use the full force of its regulatory apparatus to reclaim its housing market from the short-term rental industry.

For travelers, the practical message is straightforward: verify your accommodation's legal status before you book, be aware that the short-term rental landscape in Spain is changing rapidly, and consider that the most popular destinations in the world are increasingly choosing their residents over their tourists. That is not a bad thing — it is, arguably, long overdue.

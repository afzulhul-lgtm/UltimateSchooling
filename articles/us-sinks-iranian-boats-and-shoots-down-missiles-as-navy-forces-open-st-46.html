US Sinks Iranian Boats and Shoots Down Missiles as Navy Forces Open Strait of Hormuz

By Marcus J. Holloway Senior Tech Educator
Published: May 05, 2026 Updated: May 05, 2026 5 Min Read

As of Monday night, the US military has destroyed six Iranian small boats and intercepted Iranian cruise missiles and drones, marking a significant escalation in the conflict between the US and Iran over the Strait of Hormuz. The operation, called Project Freedom, was launched by US President Donald Trump to wrest control of the critical waterway from Iran, which had effectively closed the strait after the US and Israel started the conflict on February 28.

The move is set to have far-reaching consequences for global energy supplies, as the Strait of Hormuz is a vital shipping route that carries a fifth of the world's oil and liquefied natural gas. With the US military now actively engaged in the region, the situation is becoming increasingly volatile, and the international community is watching with bated breath.

What Happened — US Military Action in the Strait of Hormuz

According to US Admiral Brad Cooper, the head of Central Command, the US military has been actively engaging Iranian targets in the Strait of Hormuz. "The IRGC has launched multiple cruise missiles, drones and small boats at ships we are protecting. We have defeated each and every one of those threats through the clinical application of defensive munitions," he said. The operation involves 15,000 US troops, US Navy destroyers, over 100 land- and sea-based aircraft, and undersea assets.

The US military has also been working to clear a pathway of Iranian mines in the strait, and on Monday, two US-flagged commercial ships were sent through the strait to prove the safety of the route. However, Iran's Revolutionary Guards have denied that any commercial vessels have crossed the strait in the past few hours, and have claimed that US claims to the contrary are false.

Which Iranian Assets Were Destroyed and How

The US military has sunk six Iranian small boats, according to the US military, and has also intercepted Iranian cruise missiles and drones. The Iranian fast boats were sunk by US Apache and Seahawk helicopters, and the US military has been using a range of defensive measures, including electronic warfare, to defend against Iranian threats. As Cooper noted, "If you're escorting a ship, you're playing kind of one on one. I think we have a much better defensive arrangement in this process."

The US military has also been encouraging vessels to travel through the strait despite Iranian threats to use military force. "Over the last 12 hours, we've reached out to dozens of ships and shipping companies to encourage traffic flow through the strait," Cooper said. "This news has been quite enthusiastically received, and we're already beginning to see movement."

Why the Strait of Hormuz Matters So Much

The Strait of Hormuz is a critical waterway that carries a significant proportion of the world's oil and liquefied natural gas. With the US and Iran engaged in a conflict over the strait, the global energy supply is at risk of disruption. The US military's operation to open the strait is aimed at ensuring the free flow of energy supplies, but the situation remains volatile, and the international community is watching with concern.

The US blockade of Iran, which prevents ships from going to Iran or departing Iranian territory, also remains in effect and is exceeding expectations, according to Cooper. The blockade is part of the US military's efforts to pressure Iran into ending its blockade of the strait.

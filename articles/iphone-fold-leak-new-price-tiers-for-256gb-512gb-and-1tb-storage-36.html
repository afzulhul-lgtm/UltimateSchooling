The most anticipated iPhone in Apple's history is taking shape. As September 2026 approaches, leaks from multiple credible sources — including Weibo tipster Instant Digital, South Korean publication The Bell, and analyst Ming-Chi Kuo — have provided the clearest picture yet of what Apple's first foldable iPhone will look like, what it will cost, and how it differs from every iPhone that came before it.

These prices would make the iPhone Fold Apple's most expensive iPhone ever sold. The current most expensive iPhone — the iPhone 17 Pro Max at 1TB — costs $1,299. How the US Prices Were Calculated From China Leaks

A direct currency conversion of the CNY leak would put the base model at around $2,330 — but that's not how Apple prices its products internationally. Macworld's analysis showed that Apple applies a fixed-ratio conversion that historically results in a ~$330 discount versus straight exchange rates for the Chinese market.

This same methodology, applied to the iPhone 17 Pro's China vs. US pricing, produces the $1,999 starting price for the iPhone Fold's 256GB model. That figure is also broadly consistent with a Bloomberg report from March 2026 stating the iPhone Fold would be priced "north of $2,000." Most analyst consensus now sits in the $1,999–$2,349 range for the base configuration.

Display: 7.8-Inch Inner, 5.5-Inch Outer

The iPhone Fold is expected to use a book-style folding design — the same format as the Samsung Galaxy Z Fold series — rather than a clamshell flip design. When opened, it unfolds to a 7.76–7.8-inch inner OLED display with 120Hz ProMotion. When folded, users interact with a 5.3–5.5-inch outer OLED cover display.

To put the inner screen in context: an iPad mini has an 8.3-inch display. The iPhone Fold's inner screen is nearly as large as an iPad mini, folded into a device that fits in a pocket. This enables iPad-like multitasking — running two full-width apps side by side — when unfolded, while maintaining a conventional iPhone form factor when closed.

Chip and RAM: A20 Pro + 12GB LPDDR5X From Samsung

According to The Bell, the iPhone Fold will use 12GB of LPDDR5X RAM — the same amount as the iPhone 17 Pro and the expected iPhone 18 Pro. The RAM modules will be supplied by Samsung Electronics, Apple's long-time component supplier. This puts the iPhone Fold's memory configuration on parity with Apple's Pro lineup rather than any compromise.

The chip is expected to be Apple's new A20 Pro, built on TSMC's 2nm process node. The A20 Pro is also expected in the iPhone 18 Pro and Pro Max, meaning the iPhone Fold will have the same processing power as Apple's standard flagship — not a step down despite the engineering challenges of the foldable form factor.

Touch ID Replaces Face ID — Here's Why

One of the most significant and surprising details in the iPhone Fold leaks is the removal of Face ID. Analyst Ming-Chi Kuo first reported in January 2026 that the iPhone Fold will use a side-mounted Touch ID fingerprint sensor instead of the Face ID system found on every flagship iPhone since the iPhone X in 2017.

The reason is engineering. Apple's True Depth camera system — which powers Face ID — requires significant vertical space at the top of the display for the dot projector, infrared camera, and flood illuminator. In an ultra-thin foldable device where every millimeter matters for the hinge mechanism and battery, Apple apparently decided the True Depth system couldn't fit without unacceptable compromises to other components.

A side-mounted Touch ID sensor is significantly thinner and more flexible in placement. The iPhone SE third generation (2022) was the last iPhone to use Touch ID, making the iPhone Fold the first flagship iPhone to drop Face ID since the Touch ID era ended in 2018.

Face ID vs. Touch ID

Face ID is faster and works when your hands are full, but requires looking at the phone. Touch ID works with gloves, in bright sunlight, and without looking at the screen. For a foldable that users may open and use at various angles, a side-mounted Touch ID sensor may actually be more practical in daily use.

Apple's Plan to Solve the Foldable Crease

The most persistent complaint about every foldable phone — from the original Galaxy Z Fold to the latest models — is the visible crease where the screen folds. It's visible in certain lighting, it's tactile (you can feel it when swiping), and it's a constant reminder that you're using a compromised display technology.

Multiple reports, including from PhoneArena, suggest Apple is introducing a new screen technology specifically designed to dramatically reduce or eliminate the visible crease. Samsung Display — the same company that makes Galaxy Z Fold panels — is reported to be supplying Apple's foldable display panels, but with significant modifications to the hinge and display layer architecture that Apple has apparently developed to address the crease.

If Apple has genuinely solved the crease problem — or even reduced it to the point where it's not noticeable in everyday use — that alone would be a major differentiator from every existing foldable on the market, including Samsung's own flagship.

Apple is reportedly maintaining optical zoom capability, but the periscope telephoto system found in the Pro Max may not be present.

Apple's Ambitious 20 Million Unit Target

Apple is reportedly targeting up to 20 million units for the iPhone Fold's first year — a figure that has raised eyebrows across the industry. For comparison, Samsung aims to sell roughly 7 million foldables in 2026 across its entire foldable lineup (Z Fold, Z Flip, and Tri-Fold combined). Samsung is the dominant player in the foldable market outside China.

Tom's Guide described the 20 million target as "absolutely bonkers," noting that it implies Apple expects its first foldable to outsell Samsung's entire foldable portfolio by nearly 3x. Whether this reflects genuine supply chain orders or is an aspirational planning figure is unclear, but it suggests Apple believes it can — with its brand, its existing iPhone user base, and a potentially crease-free display — rapidly expand what has been a niche premium market into something much larger.

Should You Wait for the iPhone Fold?

If you're an iPhone user considering an upgrade, the September 2026 announcement is worth waiting for — but only if a $2,000+ price point is realistic for your budget. The iPhone Fold is shaping up as a genuinely new product category, not just an expensive iPhone. The combination of a near-iPad-sized inner display, the same A20 Pro chip as the standard flagships, and potentially the first foldable without a visible crease makes it compelling from a hardware perspective.

However, first-generation foldables — even from established manufacturers — have historically had durability and software optimization issues in the first year. Apple's typical software polish may mitigate this, but the foldable hinge and display are new territory for Apple. If you're risk-averse, the iPhone 18 Pro Max at $1,299 will likely offer a better camera system and known-quantity reliability at a significantly lower price.

Conclusion

The iPhone Fold is shaping up to be the most ambitious product Apple has shipped since the original iPhone in 2007. It combines a fundamentally new form factor with Apple's best silicon, a premium camera system, and — if the crease rumors are accurate — a solution to the single biggest hardware problem that has held back foldable phones since Samsung launched the category in 2019.

The price will filter out the majority of buyers. At $1,999 minimum, this is not a mass-market product. It is Apple's bid to create and own the ultra-premium foldable category in the same way the original iPhone created the modern smartphone market — setting a standard that competitors then spend years trying to match.

Whether 20 million people will pay $2,000+ for the privilege of owning the first-ever foldable iPhone will be answered in September. For now, the leaks tell the story of a device that is less an upgrade and more a genuinely new kind of Apple product — part iPhone, part iPad, priced like a statement.
